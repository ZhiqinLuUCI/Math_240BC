Section 5.4 The lower bound of \(\lambda_2 - \lambda _1\)

Let \(\Omega\) be a smooth bounded domain in \(\mathbb{R}^n , \lambda _1 \lambda _2\) be the first two eigenvalues with respect to the Dirichlet boundary condition. Then

\begin{equation*} \lambda _2 - \lambda _1 \geq \frac{\bar{\pi}^2}{4 d ^2} \end{equation*}

where \(d\) is the diameter.

\begin{proof} Let \(u_2, u_1\) be the second and the first eigenfunctions, respectively. By the variational principle, we must have \(u_1 (x) > 0\text{.}\) Furthermore, near the boundary, we must have \(\nabla u_1 (x) \neq 0\text{.}\) Thus the function

\begin{equation*} v = \frac{u_2}{u_1} \end{equation*}

is smooth up to the boundary. By a simple computation, we obtained

\begin{equation*} \Delta v = - \lambda v - 2 ( \nabla v \cdot \nabla \log u_1) \end{equation*}

where \(\lambda = \lambda _2 - \lambda _1 > 0\text{.}\) Let \(G\) be the function \(\bar{\Omega} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\)

\begin{equation*} G = |\nabla v | ^2 + \lambda (u - v ) ^2 \; \mu > \sup v\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then \(G\) is a smooth function. Let \(x_0 \in \bar{\Omega}\) be the maximum point of \(G\text{.}\) Then we claim that

\begin{equation*} G \leq \sup_{\Omega} \lambda ( u - v )^2\text{.} \end{equation*}

In fact, if \(x_0 \in \partial \Omega\text{.}\) By choosing an orthonormal frame \(\{ \ln_1 , \ldots, \ln_n \}\) such that \(\ln_1\) be the out normal direction if we let \(\frac{\ln_1}{\partial \Omega} = \frac{\partial }{\partial x _1}\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial G}{\partial x _1} (x_0) = 2 \sum^n_{i=1} v_i v_{i1} - 2 \lambda v _1 (\mu - v )\text{.} \end{equation*}

We claim that

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial G}{\partial x _1} (x_0) \geq 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

To see this, we first observed that \(\frac{\partial v }{\partial x _1} = 0\text{.}\) This can be proved using the variational principle. Thus

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial G}{\partial x _1} (x_0) = 2 \sum^n_{i=2} v_i v_{i1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Using the definition of the second fundamental form, we get

\begin{equation*} v _{i1} = - \sum h_{ij} v _j\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus we have

\begin{equation*} 0 \leq \frac{\partial G}{\partial x _1} (x_0) = -2 \sum h_{ij} v_{j}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(\Omega\) is assumed to be convex, all \(v_i = 0\text{.}\) Thus

\begin{equation*} G(x) \leq \sup_{x \in \bar{\Omega}} \lambda ( \mu - v )^2\text{.} \end{equation*}

Next we assume that \(x_0 \in \Omega\text{.}\) Then at the maximum point, we have

\begin{equation*} \nabla G (x_0) = 0 , \; \Delta G (x_0) \leq 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

We apply the standard gradient estimate: assume that at \(x_o, \nabla v \neq 0\text{.}\) Then we have

\begin{align*} 0 \amp = \amp G_i (x_0) = 2 v_j v_{ji} - 2 \lambda ( \mu - v ) v _i\\ 0 \geq \Delta G \amp = \amp 2 \sum v ^2 _{ij} + 2 v _j v_{jii} + 2 \lambda \sum v ^2_i - 2 \lambda ( \mu - v) \sum v_{ii} \text{.} \end{align*}

Since \(\nabla v \neq 0\text{,}\) we can choose local orthonormal frame such that \(v _1 \neq 0 , v _i = 0 (i > 1)\text{.}\) Thus at \(x_0\text{,}\) we have

\begin{align*} \amp \amp v _{11} (x_0) = \lambda ( \mu - v )\\ \amp \amp v_{i1} (x_0) = 0 \; 2 \leq i \leq n \text{.} \end{align*}

Thus

\begin{equation*} 0 \geq \Delta G = 2 \sum v^2_{ij} + 2 \lambda ( \mu - v) v - 4 v ^2_1 (\log u_1 ) _{11}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Using a result of Brascamp and Lieb, \(\log u_1\) is a concave function. Thus \((\log u) _{11} (x_0) \leq 0\text{.}\) Thus

\begin{equation*} \sum v ^2 _{ij} + \lambda ^2 ( \mu - v) \nu \leq 0 \end{equation*}

or, in other words,

\begin{equation*} v^2_{11} + \lambda ^2 v (\mu - v ) |_{x_{0}} \leq 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

which is not possible. Thus we must have \(\nabla v = 0\) at \(x_0\) and thus

\begin{equation*} G(x) \leq \sup \lambda ( \mu - r) ^2\text{.} \end{equation*}

We have

\begin{equation*} \sqrt{\lambda} \geq \frac{|\nabla v |^2}{\sqrt{(\sup v - \inf v )^2 - (\sup v - v )^2}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Using the same method as in the estimate of the first eigenvalue, we get

\begin{equation*} \lambda _1 \geq \frac{\pi^2}{4d^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Remark 5.4.1.

Using the method of Zhong-Yang, Zhong-Yu was able to modify the above estimate to

\begin{equation*} \lambda _2 - \lambda _1 \geq \frac{\pi^2}{d^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

However, even the above estimate is not optimal. Van de Berg (see also Yau, Problem session) conjectured that

\begin{equation*} \lambda _2 - \lambda _1 \geq \frac{3\pi^2}{d^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The estimate is asymptotically accurate for a very thin rectangular.

Not much progress was made in the direction of this conjecture. In JFA, 176, 368–399 (2000). Banuelos and Mendez-Hernandez proved that if \(\Omega\) is a convex domain in \(\mathbb{R} ^2\) which is symmetric with respect to both \(x\)- and \(y\)-axises, then the Van der Berg conjecture is true.

For a triangle, Lu and Rowlett proved the following.

Theorem 5.4.2.

Let \(\triangle\) be a triangle and let \(d = d (\triangle)\) be the diameter of \(\triangle\text{.}\) That is, \(d\) is the longest side of the triangle. Then \(\forall C > 0\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} ( \lambda _2 (\triangle ) - \lambda _1 (\triangle )) d^2 (\triangle ) \leq C \end{equation*}

is a compact set.

Proof.

When a sequence of triangles doesn't converge, then the smallest angle must go to zero. We assume that the smallest angle is \(\alpha \pi\) and we assume that the diameter \(d = 1\text{.}\)

The key part of the proof is the following cutting lemma. Let \(P_1, P_2\) be two points on \(BC\) such that

\begin{equation*} | P_1 C| = \alpha ^3 , \; |P_2 C |= 2 \alpha ^\varepsilon \end{equation*}

where \(\varepsilon > \frac{2}{q}\text{.}\) For the sake of simplicity, we assume that \(\lt A CB = \pi/2\text{.}\) We are going to prove that the eigenvalues of \(ABC\) and \(A \theta_2 P_2 C\) are almost the same, thus cutting an acute angle won't make too much difference.

We let \(U - A \theta _1 P_1 C\) and \(U^\prime = A \theta _2 P_2 C\text{.}\)

Let \(f_i\) be the eigenfunctions for \(\lambda _i = \lambda _i (ABC), i = 1, 2\text{.}\) The height of \(V = ABC \backslash U\) is at most

\begin{equation*} ( 1 - \alpha ^\varepsilon ) \tan \alpha \pi\approx (1 - a ^\varepsilon ) a\text{.} \end{equation*}

Above we have dropped a constant factor of \(\pi\) on the right side for simplicity in the arguments to follow. By the one-dimensional Poincaré inequality

\begin{equation*} \frac{\int _u {|\nabla f_i|^2}}{\int_V f_i^2 } \geq \frac{1}{( 1 - \alpha ^\varepsilon )^2 \alpha ^2} \frac{\int _{V^{\prime}} |\nabla f_i|^2}{\int_{V^{\prime}} |f_i|^2 } \geq \frac{1}{( 1 - 2 d ^\varepsilon )^2 \alpha ^2} \end{equation*}

where as before \(V = ABC\backslash U , V ^\prime = ABC \backslash V^\prime\text{.}\)

On the other hand, we always have

\begin{equation*} \frac{\int_u | \nabla f _i|^2}{\int_u f_i^2 } \geq \frac{1}{\alpha^2} , \; \frac{\int_{u^{\prime}} | \nabla f _i|^2}{\int_{u^{\prime}} f_i^2 }\geq \frac{1}{\alpha^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We normalize \(f_i\) to be

\begin{equation*} \int_{ABC} f _i ^2 = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}

Let

\begin{equation*} \int _V f_i^2 = \beta\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then by the variational principle, we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{\beta}{(1- a ^{\varepsilon} )^2 \alpha ^2} + \frac{1-\beta}{\alpha ^2} \leq \int_V | \nabla f_i |^2 + \int_u |\nabla f _i |^2 = \lambda _i\text{.} \end{equation*}

By the asymptotic estimate of the eigenvalues using the Bessel functions, we have

\begin{equation*} \lambda _i (ABC) \sim \frac{1}{\alpha^2} + \frac{C_2 }{\alpha ^\frac{4}{3}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Therefore we have

\begin{equation*} \beta \leq \frac{\alpha ^{\frac{2}{3}} C_2 ( 1 - \alpha ^\varepsilon )^2}{\alpha ^\varepsilon ( 2 - \alpha ^\varepsilon)} \leq \frac{C_2}{2} \alpha ^{\frac{2}{3} - \varepsilon}\text{.} \end{equation*}

For simplicity in the arguments to follow, we will replace the constant factor \(C_2 / 2\) by a constant factor 1, since we are considering \(\alpha \rightarrow 0\text{.}\)

Let \(\rho\) be a smooth compactly supported function so that

\begin{equation*} \rho |_v \equiv 1, \; \rho |_{v^{\prime }} \equiv 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

We may also assume that

\begin{equation*} | \nabla \rho | \leq \frac{1}{d ^\varepsilon} , |\Delta \rho |, | \Delta \rho^2 |\leq \frac{1}{d ^{ 2 \varepsilon }}\text{.} \end{equation*}

For the arguments to follow, we use the sign convention for Euclidean Laplacian so that \(- \Delta\) has positive spectrum. Note that

\begin{equation*} - ( \rho f_i) \Delta ( \rho f_i) = \lambda _i \rho ^2 f _i ^2 - f _i ^2 \rho \Delta \rho - 2 f _i \rho \nabla \rho \nabla f _i\text{.} \end{equation*}

In the estimates to follow, we will absorb all constants multiplying factors of \(a ^\delta\) for \(\delta > 0\) into a factor of one, since it is clear that as \(\alpha \rightarrow 0\text{,}\) no generality is lost by this assumption.

1. Estimate for \(\lambda _i (v ^\prime)\text{.}\) We may use \(\rho f_i\) as a test function for the Rayleigh quotient on \(v ^\prime\) to estimate \(\lambda _1 (v^\prime)\) for above. By (\(\star\)), we have

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 (v ^\prime) \leq \lambda _1 (ABC) + \frac{\int_{v^{\prime}} - \rho \Delta \rho f _1^2 - 2 \rho \nabla \rho f _1 \nabla f_1}{\int _{v^{\prime}} \rho ^2 f ^2_1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since

\begin{equation*} \int _{v^{\prime}} \rho \nabla \rho f _1 \nabla f_1 = \frac{1}{2} \int _{v^{\prime}} \nabla \rho^2 \nabla f_1^2 = - \frac{1}{2} \int _{v^{\prime}} \Delta \rho^2 f_1^2 \end{equation*}

and \(\nabla \rho, \Delta \rho = 0\) on \(v\text{,}\) we must have

\begin{equation*} \int _{u^{\prime}} -\rho \Delta \rho f _1^2 - 2 \rho\nabla \rho f_1 \nabla f _1 \leq \frac{1}{d^{2 \varepsilon}} \int _{v^{\prime} - v} f _1^2 \leq \alpha ^{\frac{2}{3} - 3 \varepsilon}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Noting that

\begin{equation*} \int _{u^{\prime}} \rho ^2 f ^2_1 \geq \int_u \rho^2 f ^2_1 \geq 1 - \beta \geq 1 - \alpha ^{\frac{2}{3} - \gamma} \end{equation*}

we then have

\begin{equation*} \lambda _1 (v^\prime) \leq \lambda _1 (ABC) + \frac{\alpha ^{\frac{2}{3} - 3 \varepsilon}}{1 - \alpha _{\frac{2}{3} - \varepsilon}} \leq \lambda _1 (ABC) + \alpha ^{ \frac{2}{3} - 3 \varepsilon}\text{.} \end{equation*}

By modifying the above argument, we are able to prove that

\begin{equation*} \lambda _2 (v ^\prime) - \lambda _2 (ABC) \leq \alpha ^{ \frac{2}{3} - 3 \varepsilon} + ( \lambda _2 - \lambda _1) \alpha ^{ \frac{2}{3} - 3 \varepsilon}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Solving the above inequality, we have

\begin{equation*} \lambda _2 - \lambda _1 \geq \lambda _2 (v ^\prime) - \lambda _1 ( v ^\prime) - 0 (\alpha ^{ \frac{2}{3} - 3 \varepsilon} )\text{.} \end{equation*}

By the gap theorem, and using the fact that the diameter of \(v ^\prime\) is at most \(4 \alpha ^\varepsilon\text{,}\) we get

\begin{equation*} \lambda _2 (v ^\prime) - \lambda _1 ( v ^\prime) \geq \frac{\pi^2}{16 \alpha ^{2 \varepsilon}} \end{equation*}

the compactness theorem follows.
