Section 8.2 Heat kernel

In this section, we construct the heat kernels. That is, we are going to find smooth function \(H(x,y,t)\) such that

\begin{equation*} T_t(f)(x)=\int_M H(x,y,t) f(y) dy\text{.} \end{equation*}

For the sake of simplicity, we shall only consider the Laplace operator on functions. Moreover, the sign convention is that on the Euclidean space, \(\Delta=\sum\frac{\pa^2}{\pa x_j^2}\text{.}\) The semigroup is formally defined as \(T_t=e^{\Delta t}\text{.}\)

The main result of this section is the following

Theorem 8.2.1.

Let \(M\) be a Riemannian manifold, then there is a heat kernel

\begin{equation*} H(x,y,t)\in C^\infty(M\times M\times\mathbb R^+)\text{,} \end{equation*}

such that

\begin{equation*} (T_t f)(x)=\int_M H(x,y,t) f(y)dy \end{equation*}

satisfying

  1. \(H(x,y,t)=H(y,x,t)\text{;}\)

  2. \(\underset{t\to 0^+}{\lim}\, H(x,y,t)=\delta_x(y)\text{;}\)

  3. \((\Delta-\frac{\pa}{\pa t})H=0\text{;}\)

  4. \(H(x,y,t)=\int_M H(x,z,t-s)H(z,y,s) dz\) for any \(0\lt s\leq t\text{.}\)

Before proving the theorem, we first formally construct the heat kernel. This formal construction also outlines the proof of the theorem.

The \(k\)-simplex \(\Delta_k\) is the following subset of \(\mathbb R^k\)

\begin{equation*} \{(t_1,\cdots,t_k)\mid 0\leq t_1\leq\cdots\leq t_k\leq 1\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

For \(t>0\text{,}\) we write \(t\Delta_k\) for the rescaled simplex

\begin{equation*} \{(t_1,\cdots,t_k)\mid 0\leq t_1\leq\cdots\leq t_k\leq t\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We assume that \(U(x,y,t)\) be a function on \(M\times M\times\mathbb R^+\) such that

\begin{equation*} \limt U(x,y,t)=\delta_x(y) \end{equation*}

be the the Dirac function. Let

\begin{equation*} R(x,y,t)=\frac{d U(x,y,t)}{dt}-\Delta_x U(x,y,t)\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\Delta_x\) means the Laplace operator for the variable \(x\text{.}\) Note that formally, any function \(g(x,y,t)\) defines one-parameter family of operators \(G_t\) by the formula

\begin{equation*} G_t f(x)=\int_M g(x,y,t) f(y) dy\text{.} \end{equation*}

We use the \(R_t, U_t\) to denote the corresponding families of operators with respect to the functions \(R(x,y,t)\) and \(U(x,y,t)\text{,}\) respectively. For any \(k\geq 1\text{,}\) define the operator

\begin{equation*} Q_t^k=\int_{t\Delta_k} U_{t-t_k}R_{t_k-t_{k-1}}\cdots R_{t_2-t_1}R_{t_1} dt_1\cdots dt_k\text{,} \end{equation*}

and \(Q_t^0=U_t\text{.}\) Let

\begin{equation*} R^{(k)} (s)=\int_{s\Delta_{k-1}}R_{s-t_{k-1}}\cdots R_{t_2-t_1} R_{t_1} dt_1\cdots dt_{k-1}\text{,} \end{equation*}

and \(R^{(0)}(s)=0\text{.}\) Since the derivative of the integral of the form

\begin{equation*} \int_0^t a(t-s) b(s) ds \end{equation*}

is equal to

\begin{equation*} \int_0^t\frac{da}{dt}(t-s) b(s) ds+a(0) b(t)\text{,} \end{equation*}

we have

\begin{equation*} \left(\frac{\pa}{\pa t}-\Delta\right )Q_t^k=R^{(k+1)} (t)+R^{(k)} (t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

As a result, we have

\begin{equation*} \left(\frac{\pa}{\pa t}-\Delta\right )\sum_{k=0}^\infty (-1)^k Q_t^k=0\text{.} \end{equation*}

For \(\mathbb R^n\text{,}\) the fundamental solution of the heat equation

\begin{equation*} (\Delta-\frac{\pa}{\pa t}) u=0 \end{equation*}

is

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(4\pi t)^{n/2}}e^{-\frac{r^2}{4t}}\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(r=d(x,y)\) is the Euclidean distance of \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\)

We wish to find the the following form of the fundamental solution of the heat equation:

\begin{equation} U(x,y,t)\sim (4\pi t)^{-\frac n2} e^{-d^2(x,y)/4t}\left\{\sum_{i\geq 0}\phi_i(x,y) t^i\right\}\tag{8.2.1} \end{equation}

where \(d(x,y)\) is the distance function on the Riemannian manifold. \(U(x,y,t)\) should satisfy

  1. \(\limt U(x,y,t)=\delta_x(y)\text{,}\) where \(\delta_x(y)\) is the Dirac function at \(x\text{;}\)

  2. For any \(N\text{,}\) \(\limt (\Delta-\frac{\pa}{\pa t})U(x,y,t)=O(t^N)\text{.}\)

The function \(U(x,y,t)\) is called the paramatrix of the heat kernel.

We pick a normal coordinate system \((y_1,\cdots,y_n)\text{,}\) and let \(r=d(x,y)\) be the Riemannian distance. We identify a neighborhood of \(y\) to a small ball of \(T_y(M)\) by the exponential map. Under this map, the coordinates of \(x\) can be written as \((x_1,\cdots,x_n)\text{.}\) On the other hand, let \((\theta_1,\cdots,\theta_{n-1})\) be a coordinate system on \(S^{n-1}\text{,}\) then \((r,\theta_1,\cdots,\theta_{n-1})\) gives a coordinate system at \(y\) also, and this coordinate system is called the polar coordinates.

Checkpoint 8.2.2.

Let \(ds^2\) be the Riemannian metric. Then we can write

\begin{equation*} ds^2=dr^2+\sum_{i,j=1}^{n-1} r^2s_{ij}(x) d\theta_i d\theta_j\text{.} \end{equation*}

That is, prove that \(\frac{\pa}{\pa r}\) is orthogonal to any \(\frac{\pa}{\pa\theta_j}\text{.}\)

Let \(\psi(r)\) be a function of \(r\text{,}\) and let \(g=\det (s_{ij})\text{.}\) Then we have

\begin{align*} \amp \Delta\psi=\frac{d^2\psi}{dr^2}+\frac nr\cdot\frac{\pa\psi}{\pa r}+\left(\frac{d\log \sqrt g}{dr}\right)\frac{d\psi}{dr},\\ \amp \Delta(\phi\psi)=\phi\Delta\psi+\psi\Delta\phi+2\frac{d\phi}{dr}\frac{d\psi}{dr}\text{.} \end{align*}

We let

\begin{align*} \amp \psi=\frac{1}{(4\pi t)^{n/2}}e^{-\frac{r^2}{4t}},\\ \amp \phi=\phi_0+\phi_1 t+\cdots+\phi_Nt^N\text{,} \end{align*}

where \(\phi_j=\phi_j(x,y)\) are smooth local functions on \(M\times M\text{.}\) We assume that under our coordinate system, \(y\) is the origin‚Äâ1‚Äâ. Then

\begin{equation*} u_N=\psi\phi=\frac{1}{(4\pi t)^{n/2}}e^{-\frac{r^2}{4t}} \sum_{i=0}^N\phi_i t^i\text{.} \end{equation*}

Therefore

\begin{equation*} \left(\Delta-\frac{\pa}{\pa t}\right) u_N=\phi\left(\Delta\psi-\frac{\pa\psi}{\pa t}\right)+\psi\left(\Delta\phi-\frac{\pa\phi}{\pa t}\right)+2\frac{\pa\phi}{\pa r}\frac{d\psi}{dr}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since

\begin{align*} \amp \Delta\psi-\frac{\pa\psi}{\pa t}=\frac{d\log\sqrt g}{dr}\frac{d\psi}{dr},\\ \amp \frac{d\psi}{dr}=-\frac{r}{2t}\psi\text{.} \end{align*}

we have

\begin{equation} \left(\Delta-\frac{\pa}{\pa t}\right)u_N=\frac{\psi}{t}\sum_{k=0}^N\left[ \Delta\phi_{k-1}-\left(k+\frac r2\frac{d\log\sqrt g}{dr}\right)\phi_k-r\frac{d\phi_k}{dr}\right] t^k\text{.}\tag{8.2.2} \end{equation}

Thus in order to find the paramatrix, we set

\begin{equation*} r\frac{d\phi_k}{dr}+\left(k+\frac r2\frac{d\log\sqrt g}{dr}\right)\phi_k=\Delta\phi_{k-1} \end{equation*}

for \(k=0,\cdots,N\text{,}\) where we let \(\phi_{-1}=0\text{.}\) The solutions of the above ordinary differential equations are

\begin{align} \begin{split} \amp \phi_0(x,y)=g^{-\frac 14}(x);\tag{8.2.3}\\ \amp \phi_k(x,y)=g^{-\frac 14}(x)r(x,y)^{-k}\int_0^{r(x,y)} r^{k-1}(\Delta\phi_{k-1})\left(\frac{rx}{r(x,y)}\right) g\left(\frac{rx}{r(x,y)}\right)^{\frac 14}dr. \end{split}\notag \end{align}

Thus we have

\begin{equation*} \left(\Delta-\frac{\pa}{\pa t}\right)u_N=\frac{\psi}{t}(\Delta\phi_N)t^N\text{.} \end{equation*}

From the above, we prove that

Lemma 8.2.3.

There is a unique formal solution \(U(x,y,t)\) of the heat equation

\begin{equation*} \left(\Delta-\frac{\pa}{\pa t}\right)U(x,y,t)=0 \end{equation*}

of the form ¬†(8.2.1) such that \(\phi_i(x,y)\) are defined in ¬†(8.2.3).

\end{proof}

Let \(\eta\) be a smooth function such that \(\eta=1\) for \(t\lt 1\) and \(\eta=0\) for \(t>2\text{.}\) Let

\begin{equation*} p(x,y)=\eta\left(\frac{2r(x,y)}{\delta}\right) \end{equation*}

be the cut-off function, where \(\delta\) is the injectivity radius at \(y\text{.}\) Then for any \(N\text{,}\) we consider the function \(u_N(x,y,t)=p(x,y)u_N(x,y,t)\text{.}\) We shall prove that

Lemma 8.2.4.

For any \(N\) sufficiently large, we have

  1. \(\limt u_N(x,y,t)=\delta_x(y)\text{;}\)

  2. The kernel \(R_N(x,y,t)=\left(\Delta_y-\frac{\pa}{\pa t}\right)u_N(x,y,t)\) satisfies the estimate

    \begin{equation*} ||R_N(x,y,t)||_{\mathcal C^l}\leq C(l)t^{N-l/2-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

\end{proof}

Using ¬†(8.2.2) and the solutions of \(\phi_k\text{,}\) we know that for \(N\gg 0\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} ||R_N(x,y,t)||_{\mathcal C^l}\leq Ct^\alpha\text{.} \end{equation*}

It follows that

\begin{equation*} ||R^{(k)}(s)||_{\mathcal C^l}\leq\frac{s^{k\alpha}(\alpha!)^k}{(\alpha k)!}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since

\begin{equation*} \sum_k\frac{s^{k\alpha}(\alpha!)^k}{(\alpha k)!}\lt +\infty\text{,} \end{equation*}

our formal construction is convergent to the heat kernel.

Theorem 8.2.5.

The \(\Delta_p\) on \(L^p(M)\) is well defined as the infinitesimal generator of the heat semi-group.

\end{proof}

For any self-adjoint extension \(\tilde\Delta\) of \(\Delta\text{,}\) the corresponding heat kernel is

Theorem 8.2.6.

Using the above notations, we have

\begin{equation*} \tilde H(x,y,t)=U(x,y,t)-\int_0^te^{\tilde \Delta(t-s)}\left(\frac{\pa}{\pa t}-\Delta_x\right) U(x,y,s) ds\text{.} \end{equation*}
