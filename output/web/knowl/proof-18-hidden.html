Since \(H\) is a densely defined self-adjoint operator, the spectral measure \(E\) exists and we can write

\begin{equation} H=\int_0^\infty \lambda\, dE\text{.}\tag{2.4.1} \end{equation}

Assume that \(\lambda\in\sigma(H)\text{.}\) Then by Weyl's criterion, there exists a sequence \(\{\psi_n\}\) such that

\begin{equation*} \|(H-\lambda)\psi_n\|\to 0, \|\psi_n\|=1 \end{equation*}

as \(n\to\infty\text{.}\)

We write

\begin{equation*} \psi_n=\int_0^\infty d E(t)\psi_n \end{equation*}

as its spectral decomposition. Then

\begin{equation*} (f(H) (H-\lambda)\psi_n, (H-\lambda)\psi_n)=\int_0^\infty f(t)(t-\lambda)^2 d\|E(t)\psi_n\|^2\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(f\) is a bounded positive function, we have

\begin{equation*} (f(H) (H-\lambda)\psi_n, (H-\lambda)\psi_n)\leq C\int_0^\infty (t-\lambda)^2 d\|E(t)\psi_n\|^2=C\|(H-\lambda)\psi_n\|^2\text{.} \end{equation*}

Moreover,

\begin{equation*} (\psi_n, (H-\lambda)\psi_n)\leq C\,\|\psi_n\|\cdot\|(H-\lambda)\psi_n\|\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus the necessary part of the theorem is proved.

Now assume that \(\lambda>0\) and \(\lambda\notin\sigma(H)\text{.}\) Then there is a \(\lambda>\eps>0\) such that \(E(\lambda+\eps)-E(\lambda-\eps)=0\text{.}\) We write

\begin{equation} \psi_n=\psi_n^1+\psi_n^2\text{,}\tag{2.4.2} \end{equation}

where

\begin{equation*} \psi_n^1=\int_0^{\lambda-\eps} dE(t)\psi_n\text{,} \end{equation*}

and \(\psi_n^2=\psi_n-\psi_n^1\text{.}\)

Then

\begin{align*} \amp (f(H) (H-\lambda)\psi_n, (H-\lambda)\psi_n)\\ \amp =(f(H) (H-\lambda)\psi_n^1, (H-\lambda)\psi_n^1) +(f(H) (H-\lambda)\psi^2_n, (H-\lambda)\psi_n^2)\\ \amp \geq c_1\|\psi_n^1\|^2+(f(H) (H-\lambda)\psi^2_n, (H-\lambda)\psi_n^2)\geq c_1\|\psi_n^1\|^2\text{,} \end{align*}

where the positive number \(c_1\) is the infimum of the function \(f(t)(t-\lambda)^2\) on \([0,\lambda-\eps]\text{.}\) Therefore

\begin{equation*} \|\psi_n^1\|\to 0 \end{equation*}

by (2). On the other hand, we similarly get

\begin{equation*} (\psi_n, (H-\lambda)\psi_n)\geq \eps\|\psi_n^2\|^2-\lambda\|\psi_n^1\|^2\text{.} \end{equation*}

If the criteria (2), (3) are satisfied, then, by the two {estimates} above, we conclude that both \(\psi_n^1, \psi_n^2\) go to zero. This contradicts \(\|\psi_n\|=1\text{,}\) and the theorem is proved.

Note that for \(\lambda=0\text{,}\) \(\psi_n^1\) is automatically zero, and the second half of the proof would give the contradiction.
