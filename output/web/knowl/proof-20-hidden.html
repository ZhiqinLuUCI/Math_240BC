Outside the cut-locus and the reference points, the function \(\rho_M\) is smooth. Since \(\rho_M\) is the distance function,

\begin{equation*} |\nabla \rho_M|=1\text{.} \end{equation*}

The Laplacian can be written as

\begin{equation*} \Delta_M = \frac{1}{\sqrt{g}} \frac{\partial }{\partial x_i} \left( g^{ij} \sqrt{g} \frac{\partial }{\partial x_j}\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Under the assumption that

\begin{equation*} ds^2 = d\rho^2 +\sum_{i,j} h_{ij}\, d \theta_i d \theta_j\text{,} \end{equation*}

we have

\begin{equation*} \Delta \rho = \frac{\partial f}{\partial r}\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(f=\sqrt{\det h_{ij}}\text{.}\) By  (3.1.1), we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial r^2} + \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} \right) ^2 \geq - { Ric} \left( \frac{\partial }{\partial r} , \frac{\partial }{\partial r} \right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

The corresponding function on \(N\) satisfies the equality

\begin{equation} \frac{\partial^2 f_0}{\partial r^2} + \left( \frac{\partial f_0}{\partial r} \right) ^2 = ( n-1) K\text{.}\tag{3.1.2} \end{equation}

If \(r\to 0\text{,}\) the asympototics \(f\) and \(f_0\) are

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} \sim \frac{n-1}{ r} , \;\; \frac{\partial f_0}{\partial r} \sim \frac{n-1}{\partial r}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since

\begin{equation*} - { Ric} \left( \frac{\partial }{\partial r} , \frac{\partial }{\partial r} \right) \leq ( n - 1) K\text{,} \end{equation*}

By the maximum principle

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} \leq \frac{\partial f_0}{\partial r_0}\text{.} \end{equation*}

This proves the comparison theorem at the smooth points of \(f\text{.}\)

Solving the equation (3.1.2), we get

\begin{equation*} f_0(r) = \frac{1}{\cosh \, \sqrt K} \cdot \sinh \, \sqrt K r\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus we have

\begin{equation*} \Delta _N \rho = \frac{n-1}{\rho} k \rho \coth k\rho \leq \frac{n-1}{\rho} (1 + k \rho)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus the Laplacian comparison theorem can be written in the following more useful form:

\begin{equation*} \Delta _M \rho \leq \frac{n-1}{\rho} ( 1 + k \rho)\text{.} \end{equation*}

We shall prove that the above inequality is true even at singular points of \(\rho\text{,}\) in the sense of distribution. To see this, we let \(\Omega\) be the domain in \(X\) such that \(\rho\) is smooth on \(\Omega\text{.}\) \(\Omega\) is a star-like domain in \(X\text{.}\) Apparently

\begin{equation*} X = \Omega \cup C u t (p) \end{equation*}

where \(Cut(p)\) is the cut-locus of \(X\text{.}\) Since \(\rho\) is at least continuous, and since the measure of \(Cut (p)\) is zero, we must have

\begin{equation*} \int _X \rho \Delta \phi = \int _{\Omega} \rho \Delta \phi \end{equation*}

for any smooth function \(\phi\text{.}\) Let \(\Omega _\varepsilon\) be an exhaustion of \(\Omega\) in the sense that \(\Omega _\varepsilon \subset \Omega\text{.}\) \(\Omega _\varepsilon \subset \Omega _{\varepsilon^{\prime}}\) if \(\varepsilon > \varepsilon ^\prime\text{,}\) and

\begin{equation*} \lim_{\varepsilon \rightarrow 0} \Omega _\varepsilon = \Omega\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then we have

\begin{align*} \int_X \rho \Delta \phi \amp = \amp - \int_X \nabla \rho \nabla \rho\\ \amp = \amp \lim _{\varepsilon \rightarrow 0} (-1) \int _{ \Omega \varepsilon} \nabla \rho \nabla \phi \text{.} \end{align*}

Using the Green's formula, we have

\begin{equation*} - \int _\Omega \nabla \rho \nabla \phi = \int _ \Omega \nabla u \cdot \phi - \int _{\partial \Omega \varepsilon } \phi \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial r}\text{.} \end{equation*}

On the boundary \(\partial \Omega \varepsilon\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial r} \geq 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus we have

\begin{equation*} - \int _\Omega \nabla \rho \nabla \phi \leq \int _ {\Omega \varepsilon} \Delta \rho \cdot \phi \leq \int _{\Omega \varepsilon } \frac{n-1}{\rho } ( 1+k\rho)\phi \end{equation*}

Finally, we get

\begin{align*} \int_X \rho \nabla \phi \amp \leq \amp \lim_{\varepsilon \rightarrow 0} \int _{\Omega \varepsilon} \frac{n-1}{\rho} ( 1 + k \rho) \phi\\ \amp = \amp \int_X \frac{n-1}{\rho}(1+ k \rho) \phi \end{align*}

which finishes the proof of the theorem.
