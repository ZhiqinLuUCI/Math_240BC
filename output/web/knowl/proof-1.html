Proof

Let \(\{U_i\}\) be a locally finite cover and let \(\{\phi_i\}\) be the partition of unity subordinating to the cover. Since

\begin{equation*} d\sum_j\rho_j=0\text{,} \end{equation*}

we have

\begin{align*} \amp \int_M d\omega=\sum_{j\in I}\int_{U_j}\rho_jd\omega=\sum_{j\in I}\int_{U_j}d(\rho_j\omega), \qquad and\\ \amp \int_{\pa M}\omega=\sum_{j\in J}\int_{\pa M\cap U_j}\rho_j\omega\text{.} \end{align*}

The Stokes' theorem follows from the following statements:

  1. \begin{equation*} \int_{U_j}d(\rho_j\omega)=0,\qquad j\not\in J; \end{equation*}
  2. \begin{equation*} \int_{U_j}d(\rho_j\omega)=\int_{U_j\cap\{x_n=0\}}\rho_j\omega,\qquad j\in J\text{.} \end{equation*}

In the first case, we may assume that \(U_j\) is the unit ball of \(\mathbb R^n\text{,}\) and \({ supp}\,(\rho_j\omega)\subset U_j\text{.}\) Write

\begin{equation*} \rho_j\omega=\sum_i (-1)^i f_i dx_1\wedge\cdots \hat{dx_i}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx_n\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\hat{}\) means omit the term. Then

\begin{equation*} \int_{U_j} d(\rho_j\omega)=\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{\pa f_i}{\pa x_i} dx_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx_n\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(f_j\) vanishes on the boundary of \(\pa U_j\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \int_{U_j}d(\rho_j\omega)=0 \end{equation*}

by the fundamental theorem of Calculus.

The proof of the second assertion is similar. We assume that

\begin{equation*} U_j=\{(x_1,\cdots,x_n)\mid\sum x_j^2\lt 1, x_n\geq 0\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then we have

\begin{equation*} d(\rho_j\omega)=\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\pa f_i}{\pa x_i}dx_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx_n\text{.} \end{equation*}

If \(i\neq n\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} \int_{U_j}\frac{\pa f_i}{\pa x_i}dx_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx_n=0\text{.} \end{equation*}

With the appropriate orientation, we have

\begin{equation*} \int_{U_j}\frac{\pa f_n}{\pa x_n}dx_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx_n=\int_{U_j\cap \{x_n=0\}} f_n dx_1\wedge\cdots\wedge d x_{n-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The theorem follows from the above two equations.
