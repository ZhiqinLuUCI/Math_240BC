Proof

We first note that \(\frac{\partial h}{\partial n}|_{\partial\Omega_\varepsilon} = 0\) on the part \(y = \varepsilon\phi_1(x)^2\) can be written as

\begin{equation*} \nabla h(\varepsilon\nabla\phi_1^2, -1) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

We define \(\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon\) as follows

\begin{equation*} \widetilde{U}_\varepsilon = \phi + y^2 \nabla\log\phi_1\nabla\phi\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then a straightforward computation gives

\begin{equation*} \left\{\begin{array}{rcl} \Delta\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon + \lambda\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon \amp = \amp O(\varepsilon^2) \\ \left.\frac{\partial\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon}{\partial n} \right|_{\partial\Omega_\varepsilon} \amp = \amp O(\varepsilon^2) \end{array} \right.\text{.} \end{equation*}

From the above, we have

\begin{equation*} \int_{\Omega_\varepsilon}\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon = O(\varepsilon^2)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Let \(\alpha\) be a number such that

\begin{equation*} \int_{\Omega_\varepsilon}(\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon - \alpha) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then \(\alpha = O(\varepsilon)\text{.}\) By the variational principle, we have

\begin{equation*} \mu_\varepsilon \leqslant \frac{\int_{\Omega_\varepsilon}|\nabla\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon|^2} {\int_{\Omega_\varepsilon}(\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon - \alpha)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

However, since

\begin{equation*} \int_{\Omega_\varepsilon}\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon^2 \geqq C\varepsilon\text{.} \end{equation*}

We have

\begin{equation*} \mu_\varepsilon \leqslant \frac{\int_{\Omega_\varepsilon}\lambda\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon^2} {\int_{\Omega_\varepsilon}\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon^2} + O(\varepsilon)\text{.} \end{equation*}

and the theorem is proved.
