We pick a normal coordinate system \((y_1,\cdots,y_n)\text{,}\) and let \(r=d(x,y)\) be the Riemannian distance. We identify a neighborhood of \(y\) to a small ball of \(T_y(M)\) by the exponential map. Under this map, the coordinates of \(x\) can be written as \((x_1,\cdots,x_n)\text{.}\) On the other hand, let \((\theta_1,\cdots,\theta_{n-1})\) be a coordinate system on \(S^{n-1}\text{,}\) then \((r,\theta_1,\cdots,\theta_{n-1})\) gives a coordinate system at \(y\) also, and this coordinate system is called the polar coordinates.
