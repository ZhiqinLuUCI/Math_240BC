Section 1.2 The extension of the Levi-Civita connection

Let \(M\) be a Riemannian manifold and let \(\nabla\) be the Levi-Civita connection. We extend \(\nabla\) to all tensor fields as follows.

Definition 1.2.1.

Let \(X,Y\) be vector fields.

  1. If \(f\in \mathcal C^\infty(M)\text{,}\) then \(\nabla_X f=Xf\text{;}\)

  2. \(\nabla _XY\) is defined by the Levi-Civita connection;

  3. If \(\omega\) is a one-form, we use

    \begin{equation*} (\nabla_X\omega)Y=X(\omega(Y))-\omega(\nabla_XY) \end{equation*}

    to define the one form \(\nabla_X\omega\text{;}\)

  4. In general, let \(\{e_1,\cdots,e_n\}\) be a local frame of \(M\) and let \(\omega_1,\cdots,\omega_n\) be the dual frame. Let

    \begin{equation*} T=a_{i_1\cdots i_p j_1\cdots j_q}e_{i_1}\otimes\cdots \otimes e_{i_p}\otimes\omega_{j_1}\otimes\cdots\otimes \omega_{j_q}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    We define

    \begin{align*} \amp \nabla_X T=X(a_{i_1\cdots i_pj_1\cdots j_q})e_{i_1}\otimes\cdots\otimes \omega_{j_q}\\ \amp +\sum_{s=1}^p a_{i_1\cdots i_pj_1\cdots j_q}e_{i_1}\otimes\cdots\otimes \nabla_X e_{i_s}\otimes \cdots\otimes \omega_{j_q}\\ \ \amp +\sum_{t=1}^q a_{i_1\cdots i_pj_1\cdots j_q}e_{i_1}\otimes\cdots\otimes \nabla_X \omega_{j_t}\otimes \cdots\otimes \omega_{j_q}\text{.} \end{align*}

Using the above notations, we have

Theorem 1.2.2.

\(\nabla_X ds^2=0\text{,}\) where \(ds^2\) is the Riemannian metric.

Proof.

Let \((x_1,\cdots,x_n)\) be a local coordinate system, and let \(\Gamma_{ij}^k\) be the Christoffel symbols. We then have

\begin{equation*} \nabla_{\frac{\pa}{\pa x_k}}\frac{\pa}{\pa x_i}=\Gamma_{ki}^\ell\frac{\pa}{\pa x_\ell} \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} \nabla_{\frac{\pa}{\pa x_k}}dx_j=-\Gamma_{k\ell}^jdx_\ell \end{equation*}

Thus we have

\begin{equation*} \nabla_{\frac{\pa}{\pa x_k}} g_{ij}dx_idx_j =\frac{\pa g_{ij}}{\pa x_k}dx_idx_j-g_{ij}\Gamma_{k\ell}^i dx_\ell dx_j-g_{ij}\Gamma_{k\ell}^j dx_i dx_\ell=0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Proof.

Using the intrinsic characterization of the Riemannian metric

\begin{equation*} (\nabla_X ds^2)(Y,Z)=X(\langle Y,Z\rangle)-\langle\nabla_X Y,Z\rangle-\langle Y,\nabla_X Z\rangle=0\text{.} \end{equation*}

We also have the following result which well justifies our definition of the curvature operator.

Theorem 1.2.3.

Let \(d_{\nabla^2}\) be the operator defined by the composition

\begin{equation*} TM\overset{\nabla}{\longrightarrow}TM\otimes T^*M\overset{\nabla}{\longrightarrow}TM\otimes T^*M\otimes T^*M\overset{\wedge}{\longrightarrow}TM\otimes\Lambda^2(M)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then it is the curvature operator.

Proof.

We have

\begin{equation*} \nabla\frac{\pa}{\pa x_i}=\Gamma_{ki}^j\frac{\pa}{\pa x_j}\otimes dx_k\text{,} \end{equation*}

and

\begin{align*} \amp \qquad \nabla^2\frac{\pa}{\pa x_i}=\frac{\pa \Gamma_{ki}^j}{\pa x_\ell}\cdot\frac{\pa}{\pa x_j}\otimes dx_k\otimes dx_\ell\\ \amp +\Gamma_{ki}^j\Gamma_{\ell j}^m\frac{\pa}{\pa x_m}\otimes dx_k\otimes dx_\ell -\Gamma_{ki}^j\Gamma_{\ell m}^k\frac{\pa}{\pa x_j} \otimes dx_m\otimes dx_\ell\text{.} \end{align*}

Thus we have

\begin{align*} \amp \qquad d_{\nabla^2}\frac{\pa}{\pa x_i}=\frac{\pa \Gamma_{ki}^j}{\pa x_\ell}\cdot\frac{\pa}{\pa x_j}\otimes dx_k\wedge dx_\ell\\ \amp +\Gamma_{ki}^j\Gamma_{\ell j}^m\frac{\pa}{\pa x_m}\otimes dx_k\wedge dx_\ell -\Gamma_{ki}^j\Gamma_{\ell m}^k\frac{\pa}{\pa x_j} \otimes dx_m\wedge dx_\ell\text{.} \end{align*}

Obviously, the last term of the above equation is zero. Thus after changing indices, we have

\begin{equation*} d_{\nabla^2}\frac{\pa}{\pa x_i}=\left(\frac{\pa \Gamma_{ki}^j}{\pa x_\ell}-\Gamma_{ki}^m\,\Gamma_{\ell m}^j\right) \cdot\frac{\pa}{\pa x_j}\otimes dx_k\wedge dx_\ell\text{.} \end{equation*}

By a straightforward computation, we get

\begin{equation*} d_{\nabla^2}\frac{\pa}{\pa x_i}=-\frac 12 R_{imk\ell}g^{mj}\,\frac{\pa}{\pa x_k}\otimes dx_k\wedge dx_\ell\text{,} \end{equation*}

where

\begin{equation*} R_{ijk\ell}=-g_{js}\left(\frac{\pa \Gamma_{ki}^s}{\pa x_\ell}-\frac{\pa \Gamma_{\ell i}^s}{\pa x_k}-\Gamma_{ki}^m\,\Gamma_{\ell m}^s+\Gamma_{\ell i}^m\,\Gamma_{k m}^s \right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

As showed above, it is quite tedious to use local coordinates to compute the curvature. The natural frame

\begin{equation*} \left(\frac{\pa}{\pa x_1},\cdots,\frac{\pa}{\pa x_n}\right) \end{equation*}

may not be our best choice. For the rest of the lecture notes, in the most cases, we use orthonormal frames.

Let \(e_1,\cdots,e_n\) be local orthonormal frame and let \(\omega_1,\cdots,\omega_n\) be the dual frame. Then the Riemannian metric can be written as

\begin{equation*} ds^2=\omega_1\otimes\omega_1+\cdots+\omega_n\otimes\omega_n=\omega_1^2+\cdots+\omega_n^2\text{,} \end{equation*}

where the tensor product is understood as the symmetric tensor product.

We write

\begin{equation*} \nabla_X e_j=\omega_{ij}(X) e_i\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(X\) is a vector field, and

\begin{equation*} R_{ijk\ell}=\langle \nabla_{e_k}\nabla_{e_\ell} e_j-\nabla_{e_\ell}\nabla_{e_k} e_j -\nabla_{[e_k,e_\ell]}e_j,e_i\rangle\text{.} \end{equation*}

The Cartan's formulas are

\begin{equation*} \left\{ \begin{array}{l} d\omega_j+\omega_i\wedge\omega_{ij}=0,\\ d\omega_{ij}+\omega_{is}\wedge\omega_{sj}=\frac 12 R_{ijk\ell}\,\omega_k\wedge\omega_\ell. \end{array} \right. \end{equation*}

Checkpoint 1.2.4.

Prove that \(\omega_{ij}=-\omega_{ji}\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 1.2.5.

Verify the above formulas.

Checkpoint 1.2.6.

Prove that given \(\omega_j\text{,}\) there are unique \(\omega_{ij}\) with \(\omega_{ij}=-\omega_{ji}\) satisfying the first Cartan equation.

Checkpoint 1.2.7.

Prove that at any point \(p\text{,}\) one can choose an orthonormal frame such that at \(p\text{,}\) the connection matrix \(\omega_{ij}\) is zero.

Checkpoint 1.2.8.

Prove that, if the curvature is zero on a neighborhood, then on that neighborhood, one can choose an orthonormal frame such that the connection matrix is identically zero on that neighborhood.

In the following we give two examples of computing the curvature using the Cartan's formula.

Example 1.2.9.

On \(\R^n\text{,}\) we give the Riemannian metric to be

\begin{equation*} ds^2=\frac{dx_1^2+\cdots +dx_n^2}{(1+\frac K4\sum x_k^2)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Let

\begin{equation*} A=1+\frac K4\sum x_k^2\text{.} \end{equation*}

and let \(A_j=\frac{\pa A}{\pa x_j}\text{.}\) We take

\begin{equation*} \omega_i=\frac{dx_i}{A}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Let

\begin{equation*} \omega_{ij}=\frac{1}{A}(A_i\,dx_j-A_j\,dx_i)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then we have

\begin{equation*} d\omega_j+\omega_i\wedge\omega_{ij}=0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Moreover, we have

\begin{equation*} d\omega_{ij}+\omega_{is}\wedge\omega_{sj}=K\,\omega_i\wedge\omega_j\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus the curvature tensor is

\begin{equation*} R_{ijk\ell}=K\,(\delta_{ik}\delta_{j\ell}-\delta_{i\ell}\delta_{jk})\text{,} \end{equation*}

and the sectional curvature is \(K\text{.}\)

Example 1.2.10.

Define

\begin{equation*} S^n=\{(x_1,\cdots,x_{n+1})\mid \sum x_k^2=1\} \end{equation*}

and the Riemannian metric to be the inherited metric

\begin{equation*} ds^2=dx_1^2+\cdots +dx_n^2+\frac{x_ix_j}{x^2_{n+1}}\, dx_i\,dx_j\text{.} \end{equation*}

Define

\begin{equation*} \omega_i=dx_i-\frac{x_i}{1+x_{n+1}}\,dx_{n+1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then

\begin{equation*} \omega_{ij}=\frac{1}{1+x_{n+1}}(x_i\,dx_j-x_j\,dx_i)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus we have

\begin{equation*} d\omega_j+\omega_i\wedge\omega_{ij}=0\text{,} \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} d\omega_{ij}+\omega_{is}\wedge\omega_{sj}=\omega_i\wedge\omega_j\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus the sectional curvature is \(1\text{.}\)
