Section 5.3 first Eigenvalue

Let \(M\) be an \(n\)-dimensional Riemannian manifold with or without boundary. Let the metric \(ds^2\) be represented by

\begin{equation*} ds^2 = \sum g_{ij}dx_idx_j\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \((x_1,\cdots,x_n)\) are local coordinates. Let

\begin{equation*} \Delta = \frac{1}{\sqrt{g}}\sum\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}(g^{ij}\sqrt{g} \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j}) \end{equation*}

be the Laplace operator, where \((g^{ij})=g_{ij}^{-1}\text{,}\) \(g=\det (g_{ij})\text{.}\)

The operator \(\Delta\) acts on smooth functions. If \(\partial M \neq \emptyset\text{,}\) then we may define one of the following two boundary conditions:

  • \ding{172}.

    Dirichlet condition: \(f|_{\partial M} = 0\text{.}\)

  • \ding{173}.

    Neumann condition: \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial n}|_{\partial M} = 0\text{,}\) where \(n\) is the outward normal vector of the manifold \(\partial M\text{.}\)

By the elliptic regularity, if \(M\) is compact, then the spectrum of \(\Delta\) consists of eigenvalues

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \leqslant \lambda_2 \leqslant \cdots \leqslant \lambda_k \rightarrow +\infty \end{equation*}

of finite multiplicity.

By the variational principal, we have the following Poincaré inequality

\begin{equation*} \int|\nabla f|^2 \geqslant \lambda_1\int f^2 \end{equation*}

To our special interests, we would like to give “computable” lower bound estimates of the first eigenvalue. Here by “computable” we mean the geometric quantities like the diameter, the bounds of the curvature, etc, that are readily available.

Li-Yau [cross-reference to target(s) "li-yau" missing or not unique] discovered the method of gradient estimates to give “computable” lower bounds of the first eigenvalue. The prototype of the estimates is as follows:

Theorem 5.3.1. Li-Yau.

Let \(M\) be a compact manifold without boundary. Let \(d\) be the diameter of \(M\text{.}\) Assume that the Ricci curvature of \(M\) is non-negative. Then we have the following estimate

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \geqslant \frac{\pi^2}{4d^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Proof.

Let \(u\) be the first eigenfunction such that

\begin{equation*} \max u^2 = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}

Let

\begin{equation*} g(x) = \frac{1}{2}(|\nabla u|^2 + (\lambda_1 + \varepsilon)u^2)\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\varepsilon > 0\text{.}\)

The function \(g\) is a smooth function. Let \(x_0\) be the maximal point of \(g\text{.}\) Then at \(x_0\) we have

\begin{equation} u_j u_{ji} + (\lambda_1 + \varepsilon) u u_i = 0\tag{5.1} \end{equation}

and

\begin{equation*} 0 \geqslant u_{ji}^2 + u_j u_{jii} + (\lambda_1 + \varepsilon) |\nabla u|^2 + (\lambda_1 + \varepsilon)u\Delta u\text{.} \end{equation*}

Using the Ricci identity, we have

\begin{equation*} u_j u_{jii} = u_j(\Delta u)_j + Ric(\nabla u) \geqslant u_j(\Delta u)_j\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus we have

\begin{equation} 0 \geqslant u_{ji}^2 + u_j(\Delta u)_j + (\lambda_1 + \varepsilon) |\nabla u|^2 + (\lambda_1 + \varepsilon)u\Delta u\text{.}\tag{5.2} \end{equation}

Suppose that at the maximum point of \(g(x)\text{,}\) \(\nabla u \neq 0\text{.}\) Then we have

\begin{equation*} u_{ji}^2 \geqslant |\nabla u|^{-4}(\sum_{i,j}u_j u_i u_{ij})^2 \end{equation*}

by the Cauchy inequality. Using the first order condition, we conclude

\begin{equation*} u_{ji}^2 \geqslant (\lambda_1 + \varepsilon)^2 u^2\text{.} \end{equation*}

Putting the above inequality into \(\circledast\text{,}\) we get

\begin{equation*} 0 \geqslant \varepsilon|\nabla u|^2 + \varepsilon(\lambda_1 + \varepsilon)u^2 \end{equation*}

which is not possible. Thus at the maximum point, we must have \(\nabla u = 0\text{.}\) Therefore we have

\begin{equation*} g(x) \leqslant \frac{1}{2}(\lambda_1 + \varepsilon)\max u^2 = \frac{1}{2} (\lambda_1 + \varepsilon)\text{.} \end{equation*}

From the above estimate, we get

\begin{equation*} \frac{|\nabla u|^2}{1-u^2} \leqslant \lambda_1 + \varepsilon\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(\varepsilon\) is arbitrary, we let it go to zero and obtain

\begin{equation*} \frac{|\nabla u|^2}{1 - u^2} \leqslant \lambda_1\text{.} \end{equation*}

By changing the sign of \(u\text{,}\) we may assume that \(\max u = 1\text{.}\) Let \(u(p) = 1\) for \(p\in M\text{.}\) Since \(\int u = 0\text{,}\) there is a point \(q\in M\) such that \(u(q)=0\text{.}\) Let \(\sigma(t)\) be the minimal geodesic line connecting \(q\) and \(p\text{.}\) Consider the function

\begin{equation*} \arcsin u(\sigma(t)) \end{equation*}

By the above inequality, we get

\begin{equation*} |(\arcsin u(\sigma(t)))'| \leqslant \sqrt{\lambda_1}|\sigma '(t)| \end{equation*}

Integrating the above inequality along the geodesic line, we get

\begin{equation*} \frac{\pi}{2} \leqslant \sqrt{\lambda_1}d \end{equation*}

and the theorem is proved.

Several extensions of the above method can be obtained when

  • \ding{172}.

    The manifold has boundary;

  • \ding{173}.

    The Ricci curvature has a lower bound.

We first address the Neumann boundary condition.

Lemma 5.3.2.

If \(\partial M \neq 0\) and \(\partial M\) is convex, then \(g(x)\) doesn't attain its maximum on \(\partial M\) unless at the point \(\nabla u=0\text{.}\)

Proof.

Let \(x_0 \in \partial M\) such that \(g(x)\) attains the maximum at \(x_0\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial g(x_0)}{\partial n} \leqslant 0\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\vec{n}\) is the outward unit normal vector. By the definition of \(g(x)\) and the fact \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial n} = 0\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} u_j\frac{\partial u_j}{\partial n} \leqslant 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Let \(h_{ij}\) be the second fundamental form. Then

\begin{equation*} 0 \geqslant u_j\frac{\partial u_j}{\partial n} = h_{ij} u_i u_j \geqslant 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

If the equality is true, then we must have \(\nabla u(x_0) = 0\text{.}\)

The next question is to sharpen the Li-Yau estimates. Even for the unit circle, Li-Yau estimate is not sharp.

Let's consider the circle \(x^2 + y^2 = R^2\text{.}\) Let the parameter, or the coordinate, of the circle be

\begin{equation*} x = R\cos \theta, y = R\sin \theta \end{equation*}

Then the Laplace operator is

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{R^2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial\theta^2} \end{equation*}

As a result, \(u = \cos\theta, \sin\theta\) are the two eigenfunctions with the first eigenvalue \(1/R^2\text{.}\) If \(u = \cos\theta\text{,}\) then with the induced Riemannian metric,

\begin{equation*} |\nabla u|^2 = \frac{1}{R^2}\sin^2\theta\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus we have

\begin{equation*} g(\theta) = \frac{1}{2R^2}\text{,} \end{equation*}

and thus

\begin{equation*} \frac{|\nabla u|^2}{1 - u^2} \leqslant \lambda_1\text{,} \end{equation*}

which is not sharp.

The problem is that in general, we don't know whether the first eigenfunction is always symmetric. More precisely, if we assume that

\begin{equation*} 1 = \sup u > \inf u = -k \geqslant -1 \end{equation*}

we don't know whether \(k = 1\text{.}\) From Li-Yau's basic estimate, we can improve the estimate \(\lambda_1 \geqslant \pi^2/4d^2\) to

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \geqslant (\frac{\pi}{2} + \arcsin k)^2 d^{-2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The above inequality is essentially useless because we know nothing about \(k\text{.}\) However, using a simple trick, we can double the estimate of Li-Yau:

Take

\begin{equation*} \widetilde u = \frac{u - \frac{1-k}{2}}{\frac{1+k}{2}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then using the standard gradient estimate, we get

\begin{equation} |\nabla \widetilde u|^2 \leqslant \lambda_1(1+a)(1-\widetilde u^2)\tag{5.3} \end{equation}

where

\begin{equation*} a = \frac{1-k}{1+k}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Now the function \(\widetilde u\) is symmetric: \(\max\widetilde u=-\min\widetilde u = 1\text{.}\) Using the same method, we get

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \geqslant \frac{\pi^2}{(1+a)d^2} \geqslant \frac{\pi^2}{2d^2} \end{equation*}

Zhong-Yang [cross-reference to target(s) "zhong-yang" missing or not unique] took one more step and proved the following result.

Theorem 5.3.3. Zhong-Yang.

Let \(M\) be a compact Riemannian manifold with non-negative Ricc curvature. Then

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \geqslant \frac{\pi^2}{d^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

\end{proof}

The estimate is called “optimal” in the sense that for \(1\) dimensional manifold, the lower bound is achieved. We shall soon see that the estimate, in general, is far from being optimal.

The basic idea of the proof is still the maximum principle. From the estimate (5.3), we suspect that there is an odd function \(\phi(\arcsin u)\) such that

\begin{equation} |\nabla\widetilde u|^2 \leqslant \lambda_1(1+a\phi(\arcsin u)) (1 - \widetilde u^2)\text{.}\tag{5.4} \end{equation}

If such function \(\phi\) exists, then we have

\begin{equation*} \sqrt{\lambda_1}d \geqslant \int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \frac{d\theta}{\sqrt{1+a\phi(\theta)}} \geqslant \pi \end{equation*}

by the convexity of the function \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+x}}\text{,}\) which implies the optimal inequality.

To prove the inequality (5.4), we use the maximal principle. At the point \(x_0\) such that the equality of (5.4) holds, we have

\begin{equation*} \phi(\arcsin u) \leqslant \widetilde u - \widetilde u \sqrt{1 - \widetilde u^2}\phi '(\arcsin u) + \frac{1}{2} (1 - \widetilde u^2)\phi''(\arcsin u)\text{.} \end{equation*}

We define a function

\begin{equation*} \psi(\theta) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} (\frac{4}{\pi}(\theta + \cos\theta\sin\theta)-2\sin\theta)\cos^{-2}\theta, \amp \theta\in(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}) \\ \psi(\frac{\pi}{2}) = 1, \psi(-\frac{\pi}{2}) = -1 \end{array} \right.\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then a straightforward computation gives

\begin{equation*} \left\{\begin{array}{l} \psi '(\theta) \geqslant 0 \\ \psi - \sin\theta + \sin\theta\cos\theta\psi ' - \frac{1}{2}\cos^2\theta\psi'' = 0 \end{array} \right.\text{.} \end{equation*}

Using the maximum principle we get \(\phi(\arcsin u) \leqslant \psi(\arcsin u)\text{.}\) Since \(\psi\) is an odd function, the theorem is proved.

Remark 5.3.4.

Recently, Hang-Wang [cross-reference to target(s) "hang-wang" missing or not unique] proved that, in fact,

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 > \frac{\pi^2}{d^2} \end{equation*}

unless the manifold is of one dimensional.

The Li-Yau-Zhong-Yang estimate is still effective when the Ricci curvature is not “too negative”. Namely, let

\begin{equation*} Ric(M) \geqslant -(n-1)K \end{equation*}

for some constant \(K>0\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \geqslant \frac{\pi^2}{d^2} - (n-1)K\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus as long as the right hand side of the above is positive, the estimate is effective.

When \(K\) is very negative, we need to modify the basic gradient estimate. The following theorem belongs to Li-Yau.

Theorem 5.3.5.

Let \(M\) be a compact Riemannian manifold without boundary. Assume that

\begin{equation*} Ric(M) \geqslant -(n-1)K \end{equation*}

for \(K>0\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \geqslant \frac{1}{(n-1)d^2}\exp(-[1 + (1 + 4(n-1)^2 d^2 K)^{1/2}])\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(d\) is the diameter of \(M\text{.}\)

Proof.

Let \(u\) be the normalized first eigenfunction. That is

\begin{equation*} 1 = \sup u > \inf u \geqslant -1\text{.} \end{equation*}

Let \(\beta > 1\text{.}\) Consider

\begin{equation*} G(x) = \frac{|\nabla u|^2}{(\beta - u)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Let \(x_0\) be the maximum point of \(G(x)\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \nabla G(x_0) = 0, \Delta G(x_0) \leqslant 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since

\begin{equation*} G(x)(\beta - u)^2 = |\nabla u|^2\text{,} \end{equation*}

we have

\begin{equation*} \Delta G(\beta - u)^2 +2\nabla G \nabla(\beta-u)^2 + G\Delta(\beta - u)^2 = \Delta|\nabla u|^2\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus at \(x_0\text{,}\) we have

\begin{align*} \nonumber 0 \amp \geqslant \amp \Delta |\nabla u|^2 - G \Delta (\beta - u)^2\\ \nonumber \amp = \amp 2\sum u_{ij}^2 + 2\sum u_i u_{ijj} - 2G[(\beta - u)(-\Delta u) + |\nabla u|^2]\\ \nonumber \amp = \amp 2u_{ij}^2 + 2u_i(\Delta u_i)_i\\ \nonumber \amp + \amp 2Ric(\nabla u, \nabla u) - 2G[\lambda_1 u (\beta - u) + |\nabla u|^2]\text{.} \end{align*}

That is

\begin{equation*} u_{ij}^2 - \lambda_1 |\nabla u|^2 - (n-1)K|\nabla u|^2 - G(\lambda_1 u (\beta - u) + |\nabla u|^2) \leqslant 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

We choose a local coordinate system at \(x_0\) such that \(u_j = 0\) \((j=2,\cdots,n)\text{,}\) \(u_1 = |\nabla u|\text{.}\) Then \(u_1 \neq 0\) (or otherwise, \(G(x_0) = 0\) which is not possible). From \(\nabla G(x_0) = 0\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \left\{\begin{array}{l} u_{11} = -|\nabla u|^2 (\beta -u)^{-1} \\ u_{1i} = 0, i \neq 1 \end{array} \right.\text{.} \end{equation*}

Using the following trick

\begin{align*} \nonumber \sum_{i\cdot j=2}^n u_{ij}^2 \amp \geqslant \amp \sum_{i=2}^n u_{ii}^2 \geqslant \frac{1}{n-1}\left(\sum_{i=2}^n u_{ii}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}(\Delta u - u_{11})^2\\ \nonumber \amp = \amp \frac{1}{n-1}(\lambda u + u_{11})^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}(\lambda^2 u^2 + 2\lambda u u_{11} + u_{11}^2)\\ \nonumber \amp \geqslant \amp \frac{u_{11}^2}{2(n-1)} - \frac{1}{n-1}\lambda^2 u^2\text{,} \end{align*}

we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{2(n-1)}\frac{|\nabla u|^4}{(\beta - u)^2} - \frac{\lambda^2 u^2}{n-1} - (\lambda_1 + (n-1)K)|\nabla u|^2 - \lambda_1\frac{|\nabla u|^2 u}{\beta - u} \leqslant 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Let \(\alpha = u(\beta - u)^{-1}\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \alpha \leqslant \frac{1}{\beta - u} \leqslant \frac{1}{\beta - 1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{2(n-1)}G^2 - \frac{\lambda^2}{n-1}\alpha^2 - (\lambda_1 + (n-1)K)G -\lambda_1 G\alpha \leqslant 0\text{,} \end{equation*}

which gives

\begin{equation*} G(x) \leqslant G(x_0) \leqslant 4(n-1) \left(\frac{\lambda\beta}{\beta-1} + (n-1)K\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Let \(l\) be the geodesic line connecting \(x_1\) and \(x_2\text{,}\) where \(u(x_1) = 0\text{,}\) \(u(x_2) = \sup u = 1\text{.}\) Then we have

\begin{equation*} \log\frac{\beta}{\beta - 1} \leqslant \int_\gamma \frac{|\nabla u|}{\beta - u} \leqslant \left[4(n-1)\left(\frac{\beta\lambda_1}{\beta-1}+(n-1)K\right)\right]^{1/2}d\text{,} \end{equation*}

or in other words

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \geqslant \frac{\beta-1}{\beta}\left[\frac{1}{4(n-1)d^2} \left(\log\frac{\beta}{\beta - 1}\right)^2 - (n-1)K\right] \end{equation*}

Choosing \(\beta_0\) such that the right side above maximized, we proved the theorem.

The optimal estimate, in this direction, was obtained by Yang:

Theorem 5.3.6.

Let \(M\) be a compact Riemannian manifold.

\begin{equation*} Ric(M) \geqslant -(n-1)K,\ \ (K > 0),\ \ d = diam(M) \end{equation*}

Then

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \geqslant \frac{\pi^2}{d^2}\exp(-C_n K d^2) \end{equation*}

where \(C_n = \sqrt{n-1}\) for \(n>2\) and \(C_n = \sqrt{2}\) for \(n=2\text{.}\)

The case when the Ricci curvature is positive is also very interesting. The following theorem of Lichnerowicz is well known.

Theorem 5.3.7.

Let \(M\) be a compact Riemannian manifold. Assume that \(d\) is the diameter of the manifold and

\begin{equation*} Ric(M) \geqslant - (n-1) K > 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

for \(K>0\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \geqslant nK\text{.} \end{equation*}

\end{proof}

In seeking the common generalization of the above theorem and the Zhong-Yang estimate, Peter Li (see [cross-reference to target(s) "yangd" missing or not unique]) proposed the following conjecture.

Conjecture 5.3.8.

For a compact manifold with \(Ric(M) \geqslant (n-1)K > 0\) the first eigenvalue \(\lambda_1\text{,}\) with respect to the closed, the Neumann, or the Dirichlet Laplacian satisfies

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \geqslant \frac{\pi^2}{d^2} + (n-1)K\text{.} \end{equation*}

Here is \(\partial M \neq \emptyset\text{,}\) we assume that \(\partial M\) is convex.

Note that by Myer's theorem, we always have \(\pi^2/d^2 \geqslant K\text{.}\) Thus the conjecture, if true, will give a common generalization of the result of Lichnerowicz's and the one obtained by the gradient estimate.

In this direction, D-G Yang [cross-reference to target(s) "yangd" missing or not unique] proved that the first Dirichlet eigenvalue of the Laplacian satisfies

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \geqslant \frac{\pi^2}{d^2} + \frac{1}{4}(n-1)K\text{,} \end{equation*}

if the manifold has weakly convex boundary. He also proved that the first closed eigenvalue and the first Neumann eigenvalue of the Laplacian satisfies

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \geqslant \frac{\pi^2}{d^2} + \frac{1}{4}(n-1)K\text{,} \end{equation*}

if the manifold has convex boundary.

Ling [cross-reference to target(s) "ling" missing or not unique] was able to improve the above estimate into

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \geqslant \frac{\pi^2}{d^2} + \frac{31}{100}(n-1)K \end{equation*}

Further improvements are possible, see Ling-Lu [cross-reference to target(s) "ling-lu" missing or not unique] for example.

We end this lecture by making the following

Conjecture 5.3.9.

Let \(M\) be a compact Ricci flat Riemannian manifold such that

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 - \frac{\pi^2}{d^2} \lt \varepsilon \end{equation*}

for a sufficiently small \(\varepsilon > 0\text{.}\) Then \(M = S' \times M_0\text{,}\) where \(M_0\) is a Ricci flat compact Riemannian manifold.
