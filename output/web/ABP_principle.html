Section 9.2 Aleksandrov maximum principle

The contents of this section is essentially from (cite chen)

Definition 9.2.1.

Let \(\Omega\) be a bounded domain in \(\R^n\) with smooth boundary. Let \(y\in\Omega\text{.}\) Define

\begin{equation*} \chi(y)=\{p\in\R^n\mid u(x)\leq u(y)+p(x-y),\,\forall x\in \Omega\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then \(\chi\) defines a map from \(\Omega\) to the set of subsets of \(\R^n\text{.}\) We call it the normal map.

Here is the geometric interpretation of the normal map. Let the lower graph of \(u\) in \(\R^{n+1}\) be

\begin{equation*} \{(x,z)\in\R^n\times \R\mid x\in\Omega, -\infty\lt z\lt u(x)\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

If \(p\in\chi(y)\text{,}\) then the hyperplane

\begin{equation*} z=u(y)+p\cdot(x-y) \end{equation*}

is a supporting plane of the graph of \(u\text{.}\) \(\chi(y)\) is the set of \(p\) such that \(u(y)+p\cdot(x-y)\) is a supporting plane, and \((-p,1)\) is the normal vector of the corresponding the plane.

Definition 9.2.2.

Let \(u\in \mathcal C(\Omega)\text{.}\) Let

\begin{equation*} \Gamma_u=\{y\in\Omega\mid \chi(y)\neq\emptyset\}=\{y\in\Omega\mid \exists \,p\in\R^n, u(x)\leq u(y)+p\cdot(x-y)\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We call \(\Gamma_u\) the contact set of \(u\text{.}\)

We consider the convex hull of the lower-graph of \(u\) and let \(\hat u\) be the corresponding function. Then \(\Gamma_u\) is the intersection of the graphs of \(u\) and \(\hat u\) projecting to \(\{z=0\}\text{,}\) which justifies its name: contact set.

If \(u\in \mathcal C^1(\Omega)\text{,}\) \(y\in\Gamma_u\text{,}\) then \(\chi(y)=\{Du(y)\}\text{.}\) If \(u\in \mathcal C^2(\Omega)\text{,}\) then \(-D^2u(y)\geq 0\text{.}\) In fact, a little bit more is true:

Lemma 9.2.3.

Let \(u\in W_{{ loc}}^{2,1}(\Omega)\cap \mathcal C(\Omega)\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \chi(y)=\{Du(y)\},\qquad -D^2u(y)\geq 0, a.e. y\in\Gamma_u\text{.} \end{equation*}

Definition 9.2.4.

Let

\begin{equation*} \chi(\Omega)=\chi(\Gamma_u)=\bigcup_{y\in\Omega}\chi(y)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Example 9.2.5.

Let \(\Omega=B_d(x_0)\text{,}\) where \(B_d(x_0)\) is the ball of radiou \(d\) centered at \(x_0\text{.}\) Consider the function

\begin{equation*} u(x)=\frac{\lambda}{d}(d-|x-x_0|)\text{.} \end{equation*}

The graph of \(u\) is a cone with \((x_0,\lambda)\) as the vertex and with \(B_d(x_0)\) as the base. We have \(\Gamma_u=\Omega\) and

\begin{equation*} \chi(y)=\left\{ \begin{array}{ll} B_{\lambda/d}(0) \amp y=x_0;\\ -\frac{\lambda}{d}\cdot\frac{y-x_0}{|y-x_0|} \amp y\neq x_0. \end{array} \right. \end{equation*}

Definition 9.2.6.

Let \(\Omega\subset \R^n\text{,}\) \(x_0\in\Omega\text{.}\) Construct a cone in \(\R^n\) with \((x_0,\lambda)\) being the vertex and \(\{x\in\Omega, z=0\}\) being the base. Let \(\omega\) be the function of the graph. Define

\begin{equation*} \Omega[x_0,\lambda]=\chi_\omega(\Omega) \end{equation*}

Lemma 9.2.7.

Let \(u\in \mathcal C(\Omega)\text{.}\) Then

  1. for any \(y\in\Gamma_u\text{,}\)

    \begin{equation*} |p|\leq\frac{2\sup|u|}{{ dist}(y,\pa\Omega)},\qquad \forall p\in\chi(y)\text{,} \end{equation*}

  2. the normal map maps a compact subset of \(\Omega\) to a closed set of \(\R^n\text{.}\)

Proof.

For all \(y\in\Gamma_u\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} u(y)+p\cdot(x-y)\geq u(x),\qquad \forall x\in\Omega\text{.} \end{equation*}

We draw a ray starting from \(y\) with direction \(-p\text{.}\) Suppose the ray hits \(\pa\Omega\) at \(x_0\text{.}\) Since \(x_0-y\) is proportional to \(p\) (and in the opposite direction), we have

\begin{equation*} u(x_0)\leq u(y)+p(x_0-y)=u(y)-|x_0-y|\cdot|p|\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus

\begin{equation*} |p|\leq\frac{2\sup|u|}{|x_0-y|}\leq\frac{2\sup|u|}{{ dist}(y,\pa\Omega)}\text{.} \end{equation*}

To prove (2), let \(F\) be a compact subset of \(\Omega\text{,}\) let \(\{p_n\}\subset\chi(F)\text{,}\) and let \(p_n\to p\) as \(n\to\infty\text{.}\) Since \(p_n\in\chi(F)\text{,}\) there is a sequence \(y_n\in F\) such that \(p_n\in\chi(y_n)\text{.}\) By definition, we have

\begin{equation*} u(y_n)+p_n(x-y_n)\geq u(x),\qquad\forall x\in\Omega \end{equation*}

By passing the subsequence if necessary, we may assume that \(p_n\to p_0, y_n\to y_0\text{.}\) Then we have \(p_0\in\chi(y_0)\text{.}\)

Lemma 9.2.8.

(1). Let \(\Omega,A\) be open domains of \(\R^n\text{,}\) \(\Omega\subset A\text{.}\) Then if \(x_0\in\Omega\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \Omega[x_0,\lambda]\supset A[x_0,\lambda]\text{.} \end{equation*}

(2). Let \(d\) be the diameter of \(\Omega\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} |\Omega[x_0,\lambda]|\geq\left(\frac{\lambda}{d}\right)^nw_n\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(w_n\) is the volume of the unit ball in \(\R^n\text{.}\)

Proof.

(1) is obvious and (2) follows from the above example.

Lemma 9.2.9.

Let \(u\in \mathcal C^2(\bar\Omega), g\in \mathcal C(\bar\Omega), g\geq 0\text{,}\) and let \(E\) be a measurable subset of \(\Gamma_u\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \int_{Du(E)}g(x(p))dp\leq\int_E g(x)\det(-D^2u) dx\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(x(p)=(Du)^{-1}(p)\) is defined and is continuous outside a measure zero set.

Proof.

Let \(J(x)=\det(-D^2u)\text{,}\) \(S=\{x\in\Omega\mid J(x)=0\}\text{.}\) By the Sard's theorem, \(|Du(S)|=0\text{.}\)

We first assume that \(E\) is an open set; \(E\backslash S\) is also open. We can find a sequence of parallel, disjoint cubes \(\{C_\ell\}_{\ell=1}^\infty\) such that

\begin{equation*} E\backslash S=\bigcup_{\ell=1}^\infty C_\ell\text{.} \end{equation*}

Moreover, we assume that \(C_\ell\) are so small such that

\begin{equation*} Du: C_\ell\to Du(C_\ell) \end{equation*}

are differmorphisms. Therefore

\begin{equation*} \int_{Du(C_\ell)}g(x(p)) dp=\int_{C_\ell} g(x)J(x) dx\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus we have

\begin{align*} \amp \int_{Du(E\backslash S)} g(x(p))dp\leq\sum_\ell\int_{Du(C_\ell)}g(x(p))dp\\ \amp =\sum_\ell\int_{C_\ell} g(x)J(x) dx=\int_{E\backslash S} g(x)J(x) dx\text{.} \end{align*}

By the Sard's Theorem, \(|Du(S)|=0\text{.}\) Thus the result is true when \(E\) is an open set.

If \(E\) is a measurable subset of \(\Gamma_u\text{,}\) then there is an open set \(G\supset E\backslash S\) such that on \(G\text{,}\) \(J(x)>0\text{.}\) On the other side, since \(E\backslash S\) is measurable, we can find open sets \(\{O_\ell\}\) such that \(E\backslash S\subset O_\ell\text{,}\) and \(|O_\ell\backslash(E\backslash S))|\to 0\) as \(\ell\to\infty\text{.}\) Thus we have

\begin{equation*} \int_{Du(E)}g(x(p))dp\leq\int_{Du(G\cap O_\ell)} g(x(p))dp\leq\int_{G\cap O_\ell} g(x)J(x) dx\text{.} \end{equation*}

The lemma follows by letting \(\ell\to\infty\text{.}\)

Lemma 9.2.10.

Let \(u\in \mathcal C(\bar\Omega)\text{.}\) Assume that on \(\pa\Omega\text{,}\) \(u\leq 0\text{;}\) and there is an \(x_0\in\Omega\) such that \(u(x_0)>0\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \Omega[x_0,u(x_0)]\subset\chi(\Gamma^+_u)\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\Gamma_u^+=\Gamma_u\cap\{u\geq 0\}\text{.}\)

Proof.

Let \(p\in\Omega[x_0,u(x_0)]\text{.}\) Then by definition

\begin{equation*} u(x_0)+p\cdot (x-x_0)\geq\hat u(x)\geq 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Define

\begin{equation*} \lambda_0=\inf\{\lambda\mid\lambda+p\cdot (x-x_0)\geq u(x),\forall x\in\bar\Omega\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

By the continuity of \(u\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \lambda_0+p\cdot (x-x_0)\geq u(x),\qquad\forall x\in\bar\Omega\text{,} \end{equation*}

and there is a \(\xi\) such that

\begin{equation*} \lambda_0+p\cdot (\xi-x_0)=u(\xi)\text{.} \end{equation*}

  1. if \(\lambda_0=u(x_0)\text{,}\) then \(x_0\in\Gamma_u^+, p\in\chi(\Gamma_u^+)\text{;}\)

  2. if \(\lambda_0>u(x_0)\text{,}\) then \(\xi\not\in\pa\Omega\text{,}\) otherwise

    \begin{equation*} u(\xi)>u(x_0)+p\cdot (\xi-x_0)\geq 0 \end{equation*}

    contradicting to \(u\leq 0\) on \(\pa\Omega\text{.}\) Since we have

    \begin{equation*} u(\xi)+p\cdot (x-\xi)\geq u(x), \forall x\in\bar\Omega\text{,} \end{equation*}

    where \(\xi\in\Gamma_u^+\) and \(p\in\chi(\Gamma_u^+)\text{.}\)

With the above lemma we immediately get the following

Lemma 9.2.11. Aleksandrov type estimate.

Let \(u\in\mathcal C^2(\bar\Omega)\) and \(u|_{\pa\Omega}\leq 0\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \sup_\Omega u\leq\frac{d}{\sqrt[n]{w_n}}\left[\int_{\Gamma_u^+}\det(-D^2 u) dx\right]^{1/n}\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(d={ diam}\,\Omega\text{.}\)

Proof.

By Lemma 9.2.8 and Lemma 9.2.10, we have

\begin{equation*} |\chi(\Gamma_u^+)|\geq w_n\left[\frac{u(x_0)}{d}\right]^n\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus

\begin{equation*} u(x_0)\leq\frac{d}{\sqrt[n]{w_n}}|\chi(\Gamma_u^+)|^{1/n}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Using Lemma 9.2.9, we have

\begin{equation*} |\chi(\Gamma_u^+)|\leq \int_{\chi(\Gamma_u^+)} dp\leq\int_{\Gamma_u^+}\det(-D^2 u) dx\text{.} \end{equation*}

We can weaken the smoothness of the function \(u\) to get the following

Theorem 9.2.12.

Let \(u\in \mathcal C(\bar\Omega)\cap W_{{ loc}}^{2,n}(\Omega)\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \sup_\Omega u\leq\sup_{\pa\Omega} u+\frac{d}{\sqrt[n]{w_n}}\left[\int_{\Gamma_v^+}\det(-D^2 u) dx\right]^{1/n}\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(v=u-\sup_{\pa\Omega} u\text{.}\)

Proof.

By assumption, there is a sequence \(\{u_m\}\subset \mathcal C^2(\bar\Omega)\) such that \(u_m\to u\) in \(W_{{ loc}}^{2,n}(\Omega)\text{.}\) That is, for any \(\Omega'\subset\subset\Omega\text{,}\) if \(m\to\infty, u_m\to u\) in \(W^{2,n}(\Omega')\text{.}\) For any \(\eps>0\text{,}\) let

\begin{equation*} \Omega_\eps=\{x\in\Omega\mid { dist}(x,\pa\Omega)>\eps\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Let

\begin{align*} \amp v_\eps=u-\sup_{\Omega\backslash\Omega_\eps} u-\eps\\ \amp v_{m,\eps}=u_m-\sup_{\Omega\backslash\Omega_\eps} u_m-\eps \end{align*}

By Theorem 9.2.11, we have

\begin{equation*} \sup_\Omega v_{m,\eps}\leq\frac{d}{\sqrt[n]{w_n}}\left(\int_{\Gamma_{v_{m,\eps}}^+}\det(-D^2 u_m) dx\right)^{1/n}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(\Gamma^+_{m,\eps}\subset\Omega_\eps\text{,}\) we write

\begin{align*} \amp \int_{\Gamma_{v_{m,\eps}}^+}\det(-D^2 u_m) dx\\ \amp =\int_{\Gamma_{v_{m,\eps}}^+}(\det(-D^2 u_m) -\det(-D^2 u))dx+\int_{\Gamma_{v_{m,\eps}}^+}\det(-D^2 u) dx\\ \amp =I_{1,m}+I_{2,m} \end{align*}

Obviously, we have \(I_{1,m}\to 0\) as \(m\to\infty\text{.}\) On the other hand, we have

\begin{equation*} \varlimsup_{m\to\infty}\Gamma_{v_{m,\eps}}^+\subset\Gamma_{v_\eps}^+\text{.} \end{equation*}

In fact, if

\begin{equation*} x_0\in\varlimsup_{m\to\infty}\Gamma_{v_{m,\eps}}^+\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then there is a subsequence \(m_k\) such that \(x_0\in\Gamma_{v_{m_k,\eps}}\text{.}\) By Lemma 9.2.7,

\begin{equation*} |p_k|\leq\frac{4\sup |u_m|}{{ dist}(x,\pa\Omega)}\leq\frac{4}{\eps}\sup |u_m| \end{equation*}

for any \(p_k\in\chi_{v_{m_k,\eps}}(x_0)\text{.}\)

Assuming \(p_k\to p_0\text{.}\) Then \(p_0\in\chi_{v_\eps}(x_0)\text{,}\) hence \(x_0\in\Gamma^+_{v_\eps}\text{.}\) Thus

\begin{equation*} \varlimsup_{m\to\infty} I_{2,m}\leq\int_{\Gamma^+_{v_\eps}}\det(-D^2 u) dx\text{.} \end{equation*}

The theorem is proved.

Theorem 9.2.13.

Let \(u\in \mathcal \mathcal C(\bar\Omega)\cap W_{{ loc}}^{2,n}(\Omega)\) and let

\begin{equation*} -a^{ij}D_{ij} u\leq f\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then

\begin{equation*} \sup_\Omega u(x)\leq\sup_{\pa\Omega} u(x)+\frac{d}{n\sqrt[n]{w_n}}\left|\left|f^+/\mathcal D^*\right|\right|_{L^n(\Gamma_u^+)}\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(v=u-\sup_{\pa\Omega} u(x)\text{,}\) and \(\mathcal D^*=(\det a^{ij})^{1/n}\text{.}\)

Proof.

We know that on \(\Gamma_v\text{,}\) \(-D^2 u\geq 0\) almost everywhere. Thus we have

\begin{equation*} -a^{ij}D_{ij} u\geq n(\det A(-D^2u))^{1/n}=n\mathcal D^*\det (-D^2 u)^{1/n}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus we have

\begin{equation*} \det(-D^2 u)\leq\left(\frac{f^+}{n\mathcal D^*}\right)^n\text{,} \end{equation*}

and the theorem is proved.

Theorem 9.2.14. Aleksandrov maximum principle.

Let \(u\in \mathcal C(\bar\Omega)\cap W_{{ loc}}^{2,n}(\Omega)\) such that

\begin{equation*} Lu\leq f\text{,} \end{equation*}

where

\begin{equation*} Lu=-a^{ij}D_{ij} u+b^i D_i u+cu=f\text{.} \end{equation*}

Assume that

\begin{align*} \amp (a^{ij})\geq 0\qquad \text{ on } \Omega\\ \amp \sum_i\left|\left|\frac{b^i}{\mathcal D^*}\right|\right|_{L^n(\Omega)}\leq\beta\\ \amp c\geq 0 \end{align*}

Then

\begin{equation*} \sup_\Omega u\leq\sup_{\pa\Omega} u^++C\left|\left|\frac{b^i}{\mathcal D^*}\right|\right|_{L^n(\Gamma_u)}\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(C\) only depends on \(n,\beta\) and \({ diam}\,\Omega\text{.}\)
