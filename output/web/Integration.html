Section 1.1 Integration on manifolds

Let let \(M\) be an \(n\)-dimensional manifold and let \(\omega\) be an \(n\)-form on \(M\) with compact support. We define the integration of \(\omega\) over \(M\) by the following way.

Let \(\{ U_j\}\) be a locally finite cover of \(M\) and let \(\{\rho_j\}\) be the partition of unity subordinating to the cover. That is

  1. \(\rho_j\geq 0\text{;}\)

  2. \({\rm supp}\,\rho_i\subset U_j\) for all \(j\text{;}\)

  3. \(\sum _j\rho_j=1\text{.}\)

Then we define

\begin{equation*} \int_M\omega=\sum_j\int_{U_j}\rho_j\omega\text{,} \end{equation*}

where the right hand side is a sum of integrations on Euclidean space, hence well defined.

Additive Principle.

The additive principle states that if event \(A\) can occur in \(m\) ways, and event \(B\) can occur in \(n\) disjoint ways, then the event ‚Äú\(A\) or \(B\)‚Äù can occur in \(m + n\) ways.

Checkpoint 1.1.1.

Prove that the definition of integral is independent to the choices of cover and the partition of unity.

The definition of integration can be generalized to manifold with boundary. A manifold with boundary is a topological space such that

  1. It can be covered by \(\{U_j\}\text{,}\) where \(U_j\) is either the unit ball of \(\mathbb R^n\text{,}\) or the upper half of the unit ball of \(\mathbb R^n\) defined by

    \begin{equation*} \{(x_1,\cdots,x_n)\mid\sum x_i^2\lt 1,x_n\geq 0\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

  2. The transition functions are smooth (up to the boundary).

Let \(M\) be a manifold with boundary. Let \(\pa M\) be the boundary of \(M\text{.}\) Then we have

  1. \(\pa M\) is a smooth manifold;

  2. \(\pa M\) is a manifold without boundary. That is \(\pa\pa M=0\text{,}\) or \(\pa^2=0\text{.}\)

The theorem of partition of unity can be generalized into the following setting.

Theorem 1.1.2.

Let \(\{U_j\}_I\) be a locally finite cover of \(M\text{.}\) Then there exists a partition of unity subordinating to the cover \(\{\rho_j\}\text{.}\) Moreover, let

\begin{equation*} J=\{j\in I\mid U_j\cap \pa M=\emptyset\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then

\begin{equation*} \{U_j\cap\pa M\}_{j\in J},\qquad \rho_j|_{\pa M\cap U_j} \end{equation*}

is a partition of unity subordinating to the corresponding cover.

Checkpoint 1.1.3.

Prove the above theorem.

A manifold is called orientable, if there is a cover \(\{U_j\}\) on \(M\) such that if \(x_j^1,\cdots,x_j^n\) are the coordinates on \(U_j\text{,}\) then we have

\begin{equation*} \det\frac{\pa (x_j^1,\cdots,x_j^n)}{\pa(x_i^1,\cdots,x_i^n)}>0 \end{equation*}

for any \(U_i\cap U_j\neq\emptyset\text{.}\) Such a set of coordinate charts is called an orientation of the manifold.

Checkpoint 1.1.4.

If \(M\) is an orientable manifold with boundary, prove that \(\pa M\) is also an orientable manifold.

Theorem 1.1.5. Stokes' Theorem.

Let \(M\) be an orientable manifold with the given orientation. Then there is a natural orientation on \(\pa M\) such that

\begin{equation*} \int_M d\omega=\int_{\pa M}\omega \end{equation*}

for any \((n-1)\) forms on \(M\text{.}\)

Proof.

Let \(\{U_i\}\) be a locally finite cover and let \(\{\phi_i\}\) be the partition of unity subordinating to the cover. Since

\begin{equation*} d\sum_j\rho_j=0\text{,} \end{equation*}

we have

\begin{align*} \amp \int_M d\omega=\sum_{j\in I}\int_{U_j}\rho_jd\omega=\sum_{j\in I}\int_{U_j}d(\rho_j\omega), \qquad and\\ \amp \int_{\pa M}\omega=\sum_{j\in J}\int_{\pa M\cap U_j}\rho_j\omega\text{.} \end{align*}

The Stokes' theorem follows from the following statements:

  1. \begin{equation*} \int_{U_j}d(\rho_j\omega)=0,\qquad j\not\in J; \end{equation*}
  2. \begin{equation*} \int_{U_j}d(\rho_j\omega)=\int_{U_j\cap\{x_n=0\}}\rho_j\omega,\qquad j\in J\text{.} \end{equation*}

In the first case, we may assume that \(U_j\) is the unit ball of \(\mathbb R^n\text{,}\) and \({ supp}\,(\rho_j\omega)\subset U_j\text{.}\) Write

\begin{equation*} \rho_j\omega=\sum_i (-1)^i f_i dx_1\wedge\cdots \hat{dx_i}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx_n\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\hat{}\) means omit the term. Then

\begin{equation*} \int_{U_j} d(\rho_j\omega)=\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{\pa f_i}{\pa x_i} dx_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx_n\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(f_j\) vanishes on the boundary of \(\pa U_j\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \int_{U_j}d(\rho_j\omega)=0 \end{equation*}

by the fundamental theorem of Calculus.

The proof of the second assertion is similar. We assume that

\begin{equation*} U_j=\{(x_1,\cdots,x_n)\mid\sum x_j^2\lt 1, x_n\geq 0\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then we have

\begin{equation*} d(\rho_j\omega)=\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\pa f_i}{\pa x_i}dx_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx_n\text{.} \end{equation*}

If \(i\neq n\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} \int_{U_j}\frac{\pa f_i}{\pa x_i}dx_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx_n=0\text{.} \end{equation*}

With the appropriate orientation, we have

\begin{equation*} \int_{U_j}\frac{\pa f_n}{\pa x_n}dx_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx_n=\int_{U_j\cap \{x_n=0\}} f_n dx_1\wedge\cdots\wedge d x_{n-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The theorem follows from the above two equations.

Lemma 1.1.7.

On an orientable Riemannian manifold, the \(n\)-form

\begin{equation*} \sqrt{\det(g_{ij})}\, dx_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx_n \end{equation*}

is globally defined.

Definition 1.1.8.

Let \(f\) be a smooth function with compact support in a Riemannian manifold. Then

\begin{equation*} \int_M fdV_M=\int_M f\sqrt{\det(g_{ij})} \,dx_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx_n\text{.} \end{equation*}
