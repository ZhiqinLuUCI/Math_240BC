Section 6.3 On the essential spectrum of complete non-compact manifold

Let \(M\) be a complete non-compact manifold. We assume that there exists a small constant \(\delta(n) > 0\text{,}\) depending only on \(n\) such that for some point \(q\in M\text{,}\) the Ricci curvature satisfies

\begin{equation*} Ric(M) \geqslant -\delta(n)\frac{1}{r^2} \end{equation*}

where \(r(x)\text{,}\) the distance from \(x\) to \(q\) is sufficiently large. J-P. Wang (cite Wang) proved the following theorem:

Theorem 6.3.1.

Let \(M\) be the complete non-compact Riemannian manifold defined above. Then the spectrum of the Laplacian \(\Delta_p\) acting on the space \(L^p(M)\) is \([0, \infty)\) for all \(p\in[1,\infty)\text{.}\)

Corollary 6.3.2.

Let \(M\) be a complete manifold with non-negative Ricci curvature, then the \(L^2\) essential spectrum of the Laplacian is \([0, +\infty)\text{.}\)

By the Bishop volume comparison theorem, we know that for any complete non-compact manifold with non-negative Ricci curvature, the volume growth is at most polynomial. In general, it is not correct to have the lower bound estimate. However, we have the following:

Theorem 6.3.3.

Let \(M\) be a complete non-compact Riemannian manifold, and let \(Ric(M)\geqslant 0\text{.}\) Then there is a constant \(C = C(n,v(B_1(p)))\) such that

\begin{equation*} v(B_p(R)) \geqslant C(n,v(B_1(p)))R\text{.} \end{equation*}

Proof.

Let \(p\in M\) be a fixed point. Let \(\rho\) be the distance function with respect to \(p\text{,}\) Let \(R>0\) be a large number. Fixing \(x_0\in \partial B_R(p)\text{.}\) By the Laplacian comparison theorem, we have

\begin{equation*} \Delta\rho^2 \leqslant 2n\text{.} \end{equation*}

It follows that for any \(\phi\in C_0^\infty(M)\text{,}\) \(\phi\geqslant 0\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation} \int_M \phi\Delta\rho^2 \leqslant 2n\int_M\phi\text{.}\tag{6.3.1} \end{equation}

We choose a standard cut-off function \(\phi=\psi(\rho(x))\text{,}\) where

\begin{equation*} \psi(t) = \left\{\begin{array}{lcl} 1 \amp \amp 0 \leqslant t \leqslant R-1 \\ \frac{1}{2}(R+1-t) \amp \amp R-1 \leqslant t \leqslant R+1 \\ 0 \amp \amp t \geqslant R+1 \end{array} \right.\text{.} \end{equation*}

By the Stoke's theorem

\begin{equation*} \int_M \phi\Delta\rho^2 = -2\int_M \rho\nabla\phi\nabla\rho = -2\int_M \psi ' \rho\text{.} \end{equation*}

By the definition of \(\psi\text{,}\) the right hand side of the above is equal to

\begin{equation*} \int_{B_{R+1}(x_0)\backslash B_{R-1}(x_0)} \rho \geqslant (R-1)v(B_{R+1}(x_0)\backslash B_{R-1}(x_0))\text{.} \end{equation*}

Combining the above equation with \(\circledast\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} (R-1)v(B_{R+1}(x_0)-B_{R-1}(x_0)) \leqslant 2n\int\phi \leqslant 2nv_{R+1}(x_0)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Obviously, we have

\begin{equation*} B_1(p) \subset B_{R+1}(x_0)\backslash B_{R-1}(x_0)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus we have

\begin{equation*} 2nv_{R+1}(x_0)\geqslant (R-1)v_1(p)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(B_{2(R+1)}(p) \supset B_{R+1}(x_0)\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} 2nv_{2(R+1)}(p) \geqslant (R-1)v_1(p)\text{.} \end{equation*}

or in other word,

\begin{equation*} v_{2(R+1)}(p) \geqslant \frac{R-1}{2n}v_1(p)\text{.} \end{equation*}

What Wang observed was the following inverse Laplacian comparison theorem: We don't have a lower bound for the Laplacian. However, we have the following:

\begin{align*} \nonumber \int_{B(R)\backslash K} |\Delta\rho| \amp \leqslant \amp \int_{B(R)\backslash K} \partial n + \int_{B(R)\backslash K} |\partial n - \Delta \rho|\\ \nonumber \amp \leqslant \amp CR^n - \int_{\partial B(R)}\frac{\partial\rho} {\partial n} + \int_{\partial K}\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial n}\\ \nonumber \amp \leqslant \amp CR^n\text{.} \end{align*}

Thus we can also estimate \(\int\Delta\rho\) from below.

Using the above observation, Wang computed the \(L^1\)-spectrum. Using the theorem of Sturm, all \(L^p\)-spectrum, in particular the \(L^2\)-spectrum we are interested, are the same.

It is possible to compute the \(L^2\)-spectrum directly, but that would be more or less the same as repeating the proof of Sturm's theorem. In fact, we can get a little more information than Wang's theorem provided.

Lemma 6.3.4.

Let \(M\) be a complete non-compact manifold with non-negative Ricci curvature. Let \(B(R)\) be a very large ball of radius \(R\text{.}\) Let \(\lambda\) be a Dirichlet eigenvalue and let \(f\) be its eigenfunction of \(B(R)\text{.}\) Then there is a constant \(C > 0\) such that

\begin{equation*} \int_{B(R)\cdot B(R-1)} f^2 \leqslant C\int_{B(R)} f^2\text{.} \end{equation*}

For the rest of this lecture we are seeking possible extensions of Wang's theorem. While we observe that Sturm's theorem is self-dual (\(M\) could be of infinite volume or finite volume), Wang's theorem is not. In what follows, we shall construct an example that all \(L^p\)-spectrum are the same of finite volume, \(L^1\)-spectrum computable, but doesn't satisfy the assumption of Wang.

The manifold we construct is of \(2\) dimensional rotational symmetric outside a compact set, and the Riemannian metric \(g\) can be written as

\begin{equation*} g = dr^2 + f(r)^2 d\theta^2, r > 1 \end{equation*}

where \(f(r) = \frac{1}{r^\alpha}\) for some \(\alpha\) large.

The Gauss curvature of \(g\) is \(-f''/f = -\alpha(\alpha + 1)\frac{1}{r^2}\text{.}\) Thus the manifold doesn't satisfy Wang's assumption.

We prove that the volume of \(M\) grows uniformly and sub-exponentially. To see this, we observe that for any point \((x)\)

\begin{equation*} v(B_1(x)) \geqslant \frac{C}{r(x)^\alpha} \end{equation*}

for some constant \(C > 0\text{.}\) On the other hand, if \(\alpha > 1\text{,}\) the volume of manifold is finite. Thus

\begin{equation*} v(B_r(x)) \leqslant C \leqslant e^{\varepsilon r}\frac{C(\varepsilon)}{r^\alpha} \end{equation*}

for any \(r \gg 0\text{.}\)

Thus the manifold satisfies the assumption of Sturm and as a result, all \(L^p\)-spectrum of \(M\) are the same.

We compute the \(L^1\)-spectrum concretely. Following Wang, we pick up a large number \(k\text{.}\) Let \(\psi\) be a cut-off function whose support is in \([1,4]\text{,}\) and is identically \(1\) on \([2,3]\text{.}\) Consider the function

\begin{equation*} g = \psi(\frac{r}{k}) e^{i\sqrt{\lambda}r}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We have

\begin{equation*} \Delta g = \Delta \psi e^{i\sqrt{\lambda}r} + 2\nabla\psi\nabla e^{i\sqrt{\lambda}r} + \psi\Delta e^{i\sqrt{\lambda}r}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We have the following estimate

\begin{equation*} \|\nabla\psi\nabla e^{i\sqrt{\lambda}r}\|_{L^1} \leqslant \frac{C}{K} (V(4k) - V(k))\text{.} \end{equation*}

where \(V(r)\) is the volume of the manifold of radius \(r\text{.}\) A straightforward computation gives

\begin{equation*} \|\nabla\psi\nabla e^{i\sqrt{\lambda}r}\|_{L^1} \leqslant \frac{C}{k^\alpha}\text{.} \end{equation*}

By the same reason

\begin{equation*} \|\Delta\psi e^{i\sqrt{\lambda}r}\|_{L^1} \leqslant \frac{C}{k^\alpha} + \frac{C}{k}\int_{B(4k)\backslash B(k)}|\Delta r|\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(dS^2 = dr^2 + f(r)^2 d\theta^2\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} |\Delta r| \leqslant \frac{\alpha}{r}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus

\begin{equation*} \int_{B(4k)\backslash(k)}|\Delta r| = \int_k^{4k}\frac{\alpha}{r^{\alpha+1}}dr \leqslant \frac{C}{k^\alpha}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Finally

\begin{equation*} \|\psi\Delta e^{i\sqrt{\lambda}r} + \lambda g\|_{L^1} = \|\sqrt{\lambda}\psi e^{i\sqrt{\lambda}r} \Delta r\|_{L^1} \leqslant \frac{C}{k^\alpha}\text{.} \end{equation*}

On the other hand

\begin{equation*} \|g\|_{L^1} \geqslant \int_{2k}^{3k} 1 \geqslant V(3k) - V(2k) \geqslant \frac{C_1}{k^{\alpha-1}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus if \(k\) is sufficiently large

\begin{equation*} \|\Delta g + \lambda g\|_{L^1} \leqslant \varepsilon\|g\|_{L^1} \end{equation*}

Thus there should be a finite volume version of Wang's theorem.

We end the lecture by some speculations of the essential spectrum.

Definition 6.3.5.

A discrete group \(G\) is called amenable, if there is a measure such that

  1. The measure is a probability measure;

  2. The measure is finitely additive;

  3. The measure is left-invariant: given a subset \(A\) and an element \(g\) of \(G\text{,}\) the measure of \(A\) equals to the measure of \(gA\text{.}\)

In one sentence, \(G\) is amenable if it has finitely-additive left-invariant probability measure.

The following theorem of R. Brooks [cross-reference to target(s) "brooks" missing or not unique] is remarkable:

Theorem 6.3.6.

([Brooks] Let \(M\) be a compact Riemannian manifold and let \(\widetilde M\) be the universal cover of \(M\text{.}\) We assume that \(\widetilde M\) is non-compact, then

\begin{equation*} \lambda_0(\widetilde M) = 0 \Leftrightarrow \pi_1(M) \mbox{ is amenable}\text{.} \end{equation*}

It would be interesting to ask

Conjecture 6.3.7.

Using the same assumptions as above. Then

\begin{equation*} \sigma_{ess}(\widetilde M) = [0, \infty)\text{.} \end{equation*}

In the case when \(\pi_1(M) = \mathbb{Z}^n\text{,}\) the above conjecture is true.

Lemma 6.3.8.

Suppose \(M = T^n\text{,}\) \(\widetilde M = \mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \sigma_{ess}(\widetilde M) = [0, \infty) \end{equation*}

Here the metric on \(M\) is an arbitrary metric.

Proof.

Let \(N\) be any finite cover of \(M\text{.}\) Let \(\lambda\) be an eigenvalue of \(N\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \lambda\in\sigma_{ess}(\widetilde M) \end{equation*}

In fact, let \(\rho\) be a cut-off function. Since

\begin{equation*} \Delta f + \lambda f = 0 \mbox{ on } N \end{equation*}

Then on \(\widetilde M\)

\begin{equation*} \|\Delta(\rho f) + \lambda\rho f\|_{L^2} \leqq \|f\Delta\rho\|_{L^2} + \|\alpha\nabla\rho\nabla f\|_{L^2} \end{equation*}

If \(|\nabla\rho|\text{,}\) \(|\Delta\rho|\) are small, then

\begin{equation*} \|\Delta(f\rho) + \lambda\rho f\|_{L^2} \leqslant \varepsilon \|f\rho\|_{L^2} \end{equation*}

If the result is not true, since \(\sigma_{ess}(\Delta)\) is a closed set, there is an interval \((a,b)\) such that for any \(N\text{,}\) there is no eigenvalues in \((a,b)\text{.}\)

We prove this by contradiction. Let \(\lambda\text{,}\) \(\mu\) be two consecutive eigenvalues such that \(\lambda \lt a\) and \(\mu > b\text{.}\) By the above argument, we can find a \(C_0^\infty\) function on \(\widetilde M\) such that

\begin{align*} \nonumber \|\Delta f + \lambda f\|_{L^2} \amp \lt \amp \varepsilon\|f\|_{L^2}\\ \nonumber \|\Delta g + \mu g\|_{L^2} \amp \lt \amp \varepsilon\|g\|_{L^2} \end{align*}

Let \(k,l\) be integers such that

\begin{equation*} \frac{k\int|\nabla f|^2 + l\int|\nabla g|^2}{k\int f^2 + l\int g^2}\in(a,b) \end{equation*}

Then by repeating \(f\) \(k\)-times and \(g\) \(l\)-times we are done.

Remark 6.3.9.

Recently, Lu-Zhou [cross-reference to target(s) "lu-zhou" missing or not unique] proved that the essential spectrum is \([0,+\infty)\) for any complete non-compact manifold with asymptotic nonnegative Ricci curvature, generalizing Wang's result.
