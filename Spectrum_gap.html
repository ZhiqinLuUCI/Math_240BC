Section 4.5 Spectrum gap of the first two eigenvalues

Subsection Heat flow proof of a theorem of Brascamp-Lieb

The following result was first proved by Brascamp-Lieb¬†[cross-reference to target(s) "blieb" missing or not unique]. In Singer-Wong-Yau-Yau¬†[cross-reference to target(s) "swyy" missing or not unique], a simplified proof was given. In this subsection, we give a heat flow proof.

Theorem 4.5.1.

Let \(\Omega\) be a bounded convex domain of \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\) Let \(u\) be the first Dirichlet eigenfunction with the eigenvalue \(\lambda_1\text{.}\) Then (up to a sign), \(u\) is positive and \(\log u\) is concave.

We begin by the following lemmas.

Lemma 4.5.2.

Up to a sign, \(u \geqslant 0\text{.}\)

Proof.

Otherwise, we may use \(|u|\) in place of \(u\text{,}\) From Kato's inequality, we have

\begin{equation*} |\nabla|u|| \leqslant |\nabla u|\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{\int |\nabla|u||^2}{\int u^2} \leqslant \frac{\int |\nabla u|^2}{\int u^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

By the variational characterizing of the first eigenvalue, we know that the right side of the above is minimized. Thus the equality must hold and \(|u|\) is an eigenfunction of the first eigenvalue.

The multiplicity of the first eigenfunction must be one. Thus up to a sign, the eigenfunction must be non-negative.

Lemma 4.5.3.

\(u > 0\) inside \(\Omega\text{.}\)

Proof.

If not, assume that \(u(x_0) = 0\) at a point \(x_0\) in the interior of \(\Omega\text{.}\) Let

\begin{equation*} u(x) = p^N(x) + O(x^{N+\varepsilon}) \end{equation*}

be the Taylor's expansion of the eigenfunction at \(x_0\text{,}\) where \(p^N(x)\) is the polynomial of degree \(N\text{.}\) From the equation \(\Delta u = -\lambda_1 u\text{,}\) we have \(\Delta p^2(x) = 0\text{.}\) Since \(u(x) \geqslant 0\text{,}\) we must have \(p^2(x)\geqslant 0\text{,}\) a contradiction to the maximal principal.

Lemma 4.5.4.

Using the above notations, we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial u}{\partial n} \lt 0 \end{equation*}

on \(\partial\Omega\text{,}\) the boundary of \(\Omega\text{.}\)

Proof.

This follows from the strong maximum principle. By the above lemma, we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial u}{\partial n} \leqslant 0 \end{equation*}

If \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial n} = 0\text{,}\) then since the function \(u\) vanishes on the boundary, we have \(\nabla u = 0\) at the point. Using the Taylor's expansion for the boundary point, we get \(p^2 \geqslant 0\) and \(\Delta p^2 = 0\text{.}\) Since \(p^2\) is harmonic, by the strong maximum principle, \(\partial p^2/\partial n \lt 0\) unless \(p^2 =0\text{.}\) But if \(p^2 \equiv 0\) then \(u \equiv 0\text{.}\) This completes the proof.

\end{proof}

We now consider the heat flow

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \Delta u + \lambda_1 u, \ \ u|_{\partial\Omega} = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

we have

Lemma 4.5.5.

For any smooth initial function \(u_0 > 0\text{,}\) the flow exists and converges to the first eigenfunction.

Proof.

Let

\begin{equation*} u_1 = \sum a_j f_j(x)\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(f_j(x)\) is the eigenfunction of the \(j\)-th eigenvalue. Then the solution of the equation is

\begin{equation*} u(t, x) = \sum a_j e^{-(\lambda_j-\lambda_i)t}f_j(x) \end{equation*}

Obviously, we have

\begin{equation*} \lim_{t\rightarrow\infty} u(t,x) = a_1 f_1(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}

If we choose \(u_0\) such that \(a_1 \neq 0\text{,}\) then the flow converges to the first eigenfunction. \(f_1(x) > 0\) by the above lemmas. By our choice of \(u_0\)

\begin{equation*} a_1 = \int_{\Omega} u_0 f_1(x) > 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

The proof is complete.

Lemma 4.5.6.

Let \(w\) be any smooth positive function on \(\Omega\) such that

\begin{equation*} w|_{\partial\Omega} = 0 \mbox{ and } \nabla w|_{\partial\Omega} \neq 0 \end{equation*}

Then near the boundary of \(\Omega\text{,}\) \(\log w\) is concave.

Proof.

Since \(w\) is a smooth function vanishes on the boundary, it can be viewed as the defining function of \(\Omega\text{.}\) By the implicit function theorem, we solve the equation

\begin{equation*} w(x_1, \cdots, x_n) = 0 \end{equation*}

to get the function

\begin{equation*} x_n = x_n(x_1, \cdots, x_{n-1})\text{.} \end{equation*}

If \(\Omega\) is convex, then

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial^2 x_n}{\partial x_i \partial x_j} > 0\text{,} \end{equation*}

is a positive definite matrix. Using the chain rule, the above inequality is equivalent to

\begin{equation*} -\frac{w_{ij}}{w_n} + \frac{w_{in} w_j}{w_n^2} + \frac{w_{jn} w_i}{w_n^2} -\frac{w_i w_j w_{nn}}{w_n^3} > 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

where \(1\leqslant i, j \leqslant n-1\text{.}\) However, if we allow \(i\) or \(j\) to be \(n\text{,}\) then as the \(n\times n\) matrix, we still have

\begin{equation*} -\frac{w_{ij}}{w_n} + \frac{w_{in}w_j}{w_n^2} + \frac{w_{jn}w_i}{w_n^2} -\frac{w_i w_j w_{nn}}{w_n^3} \geqslant 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Moreover, for any \((a_1, \cdots, a_n)\text{,}\) if \(\sum a_j w_j = 0\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} -w_{ij} a_i a_j \geqslant \varepsilon |a|^2 \end{equation*}

for some positive \(\varepsilon > 0\text{.}\) A generic vector has the form \(a+\mu b\text{,}\) where \(b = (w_1, \cdots, w_n)\text{.}\) For \(w\) small enough, we have

\begin{equation*} -w_{ij}(a_i + \mu b_i)(a_j + \mu b_j) + \frac{1}{w} |\mu|^2 |b|^4 > 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus \(\nabla^2\log w\text{,}\) whose matrix entries are

\begin{equation*} \frac{w_{ij}}{w} - \frac{w_i w_j}{w^2}\text{,} \end{equation*}

is negative definite for \(w\) small enough.

Remark 4.5.7.

The above proof is purely elementary. We can use differential geometry to give another proof. Assume \(e_1, \cdots, e_n\) are the local frame fields at the boundary point such that \(e_n\) is normal to the boundary and the rest are tangent to the boundary. Then for \(1\leqslant i, j \leqslant n-1\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \nabla^2 (e_i,e_j) w = e_i(e_j(w)) - \nabla e_i e_j w = h_{ij} \frac{\partial w}{\partial n}\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(h_{ij}\) is the second fundamental form, and \(\frac{\partial}{\partial n}\) is the outward normal vector field. Thus \(\frac{\partial w}{\partial n} \leqslant 0\text{.}\) To prove

\begin{equation*} \frac{w_{ij}}{w} - \frac{w_i w_j}{w^2} \end{equation*}

is negative definite, we write \(V=V_1 + \mu e_n\text{,}\) where \(V_1\) is tangent to \(\partial\Omega\text{.}\) Then there is a constant \(C\) such that

\begin{equation*} \nabla^2 \log w(V,V) \leqslant w^{-1}\left|\frac{\partial w}{\partial n}\right| (\pi(V_1, V_1) + C \mu \|V_1\| +C \mu^2) - w^{-2} \mu^2 \left|\frac{\partial w}{\partial n}\right|^4\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(\frac{\partial w}{\partial n} \neq 0\text{,}\) for points sufficient close to the boundary, \(w\) is small enough. Using the Cauchy inequality, we can prove the above negativeness.

Now we begin to prove the theorem: We will choose a function \(u_0\) such that \(u_0 > 0\) and \(\log u_0\) is concave. Then we shall prove that the log-concavity is preserved under the heat flow. The theorem thus follows from the above lemmas.

To construct the required \(u_0\text{,}\) we first pick up any smooth function \(w>0\) on \(\Omega\) with \(w|_{\partial\Omega} = 0\text{,}\) \(\nabla w|_{\partial \Omega} \neq 0\text{.}\) Let

\begin{equation*} u_0 = w e^{-C\sum x_j^2} \end{equation*}

for a constant \(C > 0\) sufficiently large. By the above lemma, the function is log-concave near the boundary. Away from the boundary, since \(w > \delta > 0\) for some constant \(\delta > 0\text{,}\) we can choose \(C\) large enough so that \(u_0\) is log-concave.

Using the matrix version of the maximum principle, we can prove that the flow keeps the log-concavity. Let \(\phi = \log u\text{,}\) where \(u\) is the solution of the heat equation

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \Delta u + \lambda_1 u\text{.} \end{equation*}

The flow of \(\phi\) is

\begin{equation} \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial t} = \Delta\phi - |\nabla\phi|^2 - \lambda_1\text{.}\tag{4.5} \end{equation}

By the maximum principle, if \(T\) is the first time the matrix \(\nabla^2\phi\) is generated, then there is an \(x_0\in M\) and a direction \(i\) such that

\begin{equation*} -\phi_{ii} = 0 \end{equation*}

and for other \(j\)'s, \(-\phi_{jj} \geqslant 0\text{.}\) Moreover, at \(x_0\text{,}\) \(\phi_{iik} = 0\text{,}\) \(\frac{\partial\phi_{ii}}{\partial t} \leqslant 0\) and \(\Delta\phi_{ii} \geqslant 0\text{.}\)

Differentiating¬†(4.2) by \(i,i\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} 0 \geqslant \frac{\partial\phi_{ii}}{\partial t} = \Delta\phi_{ii} - 2\phi_k\phi_{kii} - 2\phi_{ki}^2 \geqslant -2\phi_{ki}^2\text{.} \end{equation*}

By the convexity, \(\phi_{ki}^2 \leqslant \phi_{ii}\phi_{kk} = 0\text{.}\) Thus \(\phi_{ki} = 0\) for \(k\neq i\text{.}\) The theorem follows from the strong maximum principle.

This completes the proof of the Brascamp-Lieb theorem.

\end{proof}

Subsection Gap of the first two eigenvalues

For the sake of simplicity, we only consider bounded smooth domain in \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\)

Let \(\Omega\) be a bounded domain in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) with smooth boundary. Let \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\) be the Dirichlet first two eigenvalues. Since \(\lambda_1\) must be simple (of multiplicity one) we have

\begin{equation*} \lambda_2 - \lambda_1> 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

The question we would like to answer is that, how to get the lower bound estimate of \(\lambda_2 - \lambda_1\text{?}\)

Let \(\phi_1, \phi_2\) be the eigenfunctions with respect to \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\text{.}\) We set

\begin{equation*} \phi = \frac{\phi_2}{\phi_1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then a straightforward computation gives

\begin{equation*} \Delta\phi + \partial\nabla\log\phi_1\nabla\phi = -\lambda\phi\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\lambda = \lambda_2 - \lambda_1\text{.}\) Moreover, since \(\phi_1|_{\partial\Omega} = 0\text{,}\) we must have

\begin{equation*} \left.\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial n}\right|_{\partial\Omega} = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

We have a notion of Bakry-√âmery Ricci tensor. Let

\begin{equation*} f = \phi_1^2 \end{equation*}

Then the Bakry-√âmery Laplacian is defined as

\begin{equation*} \Delta f = \Delta + \nabla\log f\nabla \end{equation*}

which is a self-adjoint operator with respect to the volume form \(\phi_1^2 dV\text{.}\)

The Bakry-√âmery Ricci tensor is defined as

\begin{equation*} -\nabla^2 \log f \end{equation*}

By the Brascamp-Lieb theorem, the Barkey-Emery Ricci curvature is non-negative.

We make the following conjecture:

Conjecture 4.5.8.

The method of gradient estimates can be generalized to the Bakry-√âmery case without additional difficulties.

The above conjecture, if true, will give a unified proof between the estimation of the first eigenvalue and gap estimate.

We can go one more step further. Since the Barkey-Emery Ricci tensor comes from the setting \((M, ds^2, e^f dV)\text{,}\) which can be considered as the limit of the wrap product

\begin{equation*} e^f dr^2 + ds^2 \end{equation*}

we make the following definition.

Let

\begin{equation*} \Omega_\varepsilon = \{(x,y)|x\in \Omega, 0\leqslant y \leqslant \varepsilon \phi_1^2(x)\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Let \(\mu_\varepsilon\) be the first Neumann eigenvalue. Then

Conjecture 4.5.9.

Using the above notations, we have

\begin{equation*} \lambda_2 - \lambda_1 = \lim_{\varepsilon\rightarrow 0} \mu_\varepsilon\text{.} \end{equation*}

Similarly, we make the following

Conjecture 4.5.10.

Let \((\mu_\varepsilon)^k\) be the \(k\)-th Neumann eigenvalue of \(\Omega_\varepsilon\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \lim_{\varepsilon\rightarrow 0}(\mu_\varepsilon)^k = \lambda_{k+1} - \lambda_1\text{.} \end{equation*}

We can prove the following

Theorem 4.5.11.

\begin{equation*} \lambda_2 - \lambda_1 \geqslant \lim_{\varepsilon\rightarrow 0}\mu_\varepsilon\text{.} \end{equation*}

Proof.

We first note that \(\frac{\partial h}{\partial n}|_{\partial\Omega_\varepsilon} = 0\) on the part \(y = \varepsilon\phi_1(x)^2\) can be written as

\begin{equation*} \nabla h(\varepsilon\nabla\phi_1^2, -1) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

We define \(\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon\) as follows

\begin{equation*} \widetilde{U}_\varepsilon = \phi + y^2 \nabla\log\phi_1\nabla\phi\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then a straightforward computation gives

\begin{equation*} \left\{\begin{array}{rcl} \Delta\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon + \lambda\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon \amp = \amp O(\varepsilon^2) \\ \left.\frac{\partial\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon}{\partial n} \right|_{\partial\Omega_\varepsilon} \amp = \amp O(\varepsilon^2) \end{array} \right.\text{.} \end{equation*}

From the above, we have

\begin{equation*} \int_{\Omega_\varepsilon}\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon = O(\varepsilon^2)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Let \(\alpha\) be a number such that

\begin{equation*} \int_{\Omega_\varepsilon}(\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon - \alpha) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then \(\alpha = O(\varepsilon)\text{.}\) By the variational principle, we have

\begin{equation*} \mu_\varepsilon \leqslant \frac{\int_{\Omega_\varepsilon}|\nabla\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon|^2} {\int_{\Omega_\varepsilon}(\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon - \alpha)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

However, since

\begin{equation*} \int_{\Omega_\varepsilon}\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon^2 \geqq C\varepsilon\text{.} \end{equation*}

We have

\begin{equation*} \mu_\varepsilon \leqslant \frac{\int_{\Omega_\varepsilon}\lambda\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon^2} {\int_{\Omega_\varepsilon}\widetilde{U}_\varepsilon^2} + O(\varepsilon)\text{.} \end{equation*}

and the theorem is proved.

\end{proof}

Remark 4.5.12.

We consider the wrap product

\begin{equation*} e^f dr^2 + d^2 \end{equation*}

over \(\Omega_\varepsilon\text{,}\) we see the relation between two settings. This also gives the relation between the Barkey-Emery geometry with respect to the ordinary Riemannian geometry.

Some applications.

Theorem 4.5.13. Singer-Wong-Yau-Yau, Yu-Zhong.

Let \(\Omega\) be a convex domain in \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \lambda_2 - \lambda_1 \geqslant \frac{\pi^2}{d^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Proof.

We consider the domain \(\Omega_\varepsilon\) and let \(U_\varepsilon\) be the first Neumann eigenfunction. By what we proved in the last lecture, we have

\begin{equation*} \mu_\varepsilon \geqslant \frac{\pi^2}{d_\varepsilon^2} \end{equation*}

where \(d_\varepsilon\) is the diameter of \(\Omega_\varepsilon\text{.}\) Since \(d_\varepsilon\rightarrow d\text{,}\) the theorem is proved.

Question 4.5.14.

How to recover the recent result of Yau?

We are going to use the following result of Chen-Li.

Theorem 4.5.15.

Let \(M\) be an \(m\)-dimensional domain in \(\mathbb{R}^m\text{.}\) Let \(M\) be star-shaped. Let \(R\) be the radius of the largest ball centered at \(p\in M\) contained in \(M\) and let \(R_0\) be the smallest ball centered at \(p\) containing \(M\text{.}\) Then there is a constant \(C\text{,}\) depending only on \(m\text{,}\) such that

\begin{equation*} \eta_1 \geqslant C\frac{R^m}{R_0^{m+2}} \end{equation*}

Let \(U_\varepsilon\) be the first Neumann eigenfunction of \(\Omega_\varepsilon\text{.}\) Then asymptotically we can write

\begin{equation*} U_\varepsilon \sim \phi + y^2 \nabla\log \phi_1 \nabla\phi \end{equation*}

In general, power series method can be used to prove the conjecture.

Remark 4.5.16.

Conjecture¬†4.5.9 was proved by Lu-Rowlett¬†[cross-reference to target(s) "lu-rowlett" missing or not unique].

Subsection Appendix: Eigenvalues of collapsing domain

It is important to study the asymptotic behavior of eigenvalues when a domain collapses. In this appendix, we give some preliminary results in this direction.

We begin with the following observation. Consider the sector

What is the asymptotic behavior of the Dirichlet eigenvalues when \(\alpha\rightarrow 0\text{?}\)

As is well-known, the eigenfunctions of the sector are of the form

\begin{equation*} f(r)\sin\frac{\theta}{\alpha} \end{equation*}

where \(f(r)\) is the so-called Bessel function

\begin{equation*} f'' + \frac{1}{r}f' + \frac{1}{r^2}(r^2 - \frac{1}{\alpha^2})f = -\lambda f \end{equation*}

If we set

\begin{equation*} g(r) = \sqrt{r} f(r) \end{equation*}

Then the corresponding equation becomes

\begin{equation*} -g''(r) + \frac{1}{r^2}(\frac{1}{\alpha^2}-\frac{1}{4})g = \lambda g \end{equation*}

When \(\alpha\rightarrow 0\text{,}\) we take the following renomorization: set \(1 - r = \alpha^{2/3}x\text{.}\) Then we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{r^2} - 1 \sim 2x\alpha^{2/3} \end{equation*}

Let \(\lambda = \widetilde\lambda + \frac{1}{\alpha^2}\text{.}\) Then we have

\begin{equation*} -g''(r) + \left(\frac{1}{r^2}\left(\frac{1}{\alpha^2}-\frac{1}{4}\right)- \frac{1}{\alpha^2}\right)g = \widetilde\lambda g \end{equation*}

and if \(\alpha\rightarrow 0\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} -g'' + 2xg = \lambda g \end{equation*}

This is the Airy's function.

Friedlander and Solomyak was able to generalize the above result in the following setting:

Let \(h(x) > 0\) be a piecewise linear function defined on \([-a, b]\text{,}\) where \(a, b > 0\text{.}\) We assume that

\begin{equation*} h(x) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} M-C_+x \amp x > 0 \\ M-C_-x \amp x \lt 0 \end{array} \right. \end{equation*}

where the choice of \(M, C_+, C_-\) are so that \(h(-a) = h(b) = 0\text{.}\)

For any positive \(\varepsilon > 0\text{,}\) let

\begin{equation*} \Omega_\varepsilon = \{(x,y)|x\in I, 0\leqslant y \leqslant \varepsilon h(x)\} \end{equation*}

Theorem 4.5.17. Friedlander-Solomyak.

Let \(\alpha = 2/3\text{.}\) Let \(l_j(\varepsilon)\) be the Dirichlet eigenvalues of \(\Omega_\varepsilon\text{.}\) Let

\begin{equation*} \mu_j = \lim_{\varepsilon\rightarrow 0} \varepsilon^{2\alpha} \left(l_j(\varepsilon) - \frac{\pi^2}{M^2\varepsilon^2}\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

exists. Then \(\{\mu_j\}\) are eigenvalues of the Schr√∂dinger operator \(H\) on \(L^2(\mathbb{R})\text{,}\) where

\begin{equation*} H = -\frac{d^2}{dx^2} + q(x) \end{equation*}

where

\begin{equation*} q(x) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 2\pi^2 M^{-3} C_+x \amp x > 0 \\ 2\pi^2 M^{-3} C_-x \amp x \lt 0 \end{array} \right. \end{equation*}

Note that if \(C_+ = C_-\text{,}\) then \(H\) turns to the harmonic oscillator. If \(C_+ = +\infty\text{,}\) then it turns to the above discussed case.

Theorem 4.5.18. Lu-Rowlett.

Let \(M\) be a triangle and let \(d\) be the diameter of \(M\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} d^2(\lambda_2 - \lambda_1) \rightarrow +\infty \end{equation*}

if the triangle collapses. In other words, the ‚Äúgap‚Äù function is a proper function on the moduli space of triangles.

The original proof of the above result is independent to the work of Friedlander et.la.

Question 4.5.19.

Let \(h(x) > 0\) be a piecewise smooth function on a bounded domain \(\Omega\) of \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\) Let

\begin{equation*} \Omega_\varepsilon = \{(x,y)|x\in\Omega, 0\leqslant y\leqslant\varepsilon h(x)\} \end{equation*}

Then what is the asymptotical behavior of the Dirichlet (Neumann) eigenvalues of \(\Omega_\varepsilon\text{.}\)

The question is very important in answering the following conjecture of Van den Berg and Yau.

Conjecture 4.5.20.

Let \(\Omega\) be a convex bounded domain in \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \lambda_2 - \lambda_1 \geqslant \frac{3\pi^2}{d^2} \end{equation*}

The conjecture is asymptotically optimal for thin rectangles.

In some sense, the result of Friedlander gives the compactification of the Laplacians on the moduli space. Namely, if \(\alpha\rightarrow 0\text{,}\) then the Laplace operators tend to the Schr√∂dinger operator defined above.

As an application, we relate the result to the following conjecture.

Conjecture 4.5.21.

Does it exist a number \(N\) such that the first \(N\) Dirichlet eigenvalues determine to triangle.

The result of Chang-Deturck gives partial answer to the above conjecture:

Theorem 4.5.22.

There exists \(N = N(\lambda_1, \lambda_2)\) such that \(\lambda_1, \cdots, \lambda_N\) determines the triangle.

Unfortunately, if \(\alpha\rightarrow 0\text{,}\) \(N = N(\lambda_1,\lambda_2)\rightarrow +\infty\text{.}\)

Conjecture 4.5.23.

Let \(\xi = \alpha^{2/3}\text{.}\) Define two functions

\begin{align*} \nonumber P(\zeta, b) \amp = \amp \lambda_2 / \lambda_1\\ \nonumber Q(\zeta, b) \amp = \amp (\lambda_3 - \lambda_2)/(\lambda_2 - \lambda_1) \end{align*}

\begin{align*} \nonumber P(\zeta, b) \amp = \amp 1 + a\zeta + O(\zeta)\\ \nonumber Q(\zeta, b) \amp = \amp 1 + o(1) \end{align*}

The hearing problem is implied by proving

\begin{equation*} (\zeta, b) \mapsto (P,Q) \end{equation*}
