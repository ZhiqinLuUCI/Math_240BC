Section 4.1 Heat kernel

Let \(M\) be a Riemannian manifold. The Laplace operator \(\Delta\) can be extended as a densely defined self-adjoint operator. Thus by the spectrum theorem, we can write

\begin{equation*} \Delta = \int \lambda d E \end{equation*}

where \(E\) is the corresponding spectrum measure. Using functional analysis, we define

\begin{equation*} e ^{- \Delta t} = \int ^\infty _0 e ^{- \lambda t } \, dE \end{equation*}

to be the heat operator, and

\begin{equation*} \int^\infty _ 0 e ^{-\Delta t} \, dt \end{equation*}

to be the Green's operator.

The heat semi-group, or the heat operator, has a kernel.

Theorem 4.1.1.

Let \(M\) be a complete Riemannian, then there is a heat kernel \(H (x, y, t) \in C^\infty (M \times M \times \mathbb{R} ^+ )\) such that

\begin{equation*} (e ^{- \Delta t} f) (x) = \int_M H (x, y, t )f (y) \end{equation*}

\(\forall f \in L^2 (M)\) such that

  1. { (1)}.

    \(\displaystyle H(x, y, t) = H (y, x, t)\)

  2. { (2)}.

    \(\displaystyle \lim_{t \rightarrow 0^{+} } H (x, y, t) = \delta _x (y)\)

  3. { (3)}.

    \(\displaystyle \left( \Delta - \frac{\partial }{\partial t} \right) H = 0\)

  4. { (4)}.

    \(H(x, y, t) = \int H (x, z, t - s) H (z, y, s) \, dz\text{.}\)

Remark 4.1.2.

Let \(M\) be a compact manifold and let \(\{ f_i\}\) be an orthonormal basis of eigenfunctions. Let \(\lambda _i\) be the corresponding eigenvalues. Then

\begin{equation*} H(x, y, t) = \sum^\infty_{k=1} e ^{-\lambda _{k} t} f _k (x) f_k (y)\text{.} \end{equation*}

As in the case of harmonic functions, for positive solutions of the heat equations

\begin{equation*} \left( \Delta - \frac{\partial }{\partial t} \right) u= 0 \end{equation*}

we also have the differentiable Harnack inequality. The theorem is as follows:

Theorem 4.1.3.

Let \(M\) be a compact Riemannian manifold with boundary, Ric\((M) \geq 0\text{.}\) If \(\partial M \neq \emptyset\text{,}\) we assume that \(\partial M\) is convex. In this case, we assume that \(u (x, t)\) satisfies the Neumann boundary.

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial u}{\partial n} = 0 \end{equation*}

on \(\partial M x (0, \infty)\text{,}\) where \(\frac{\partial }{\partial n}\) is the outer normal direction. Then on \(M x (0, \infty)\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{|\nabla u|^2}{u^2} - \frac{ut}{u} \leq \frac{n}{2t}\text{.} \end{equation*}

For manifold with Ric\((M) \geq - k\text{,}\) the estimates are more complicated but the same principle applies.

The Harnack inequalities follows from the gradient estimates:

Using the gradient estimates and the Harnack inequality we obtain:

Theorem 4.1.4.

Let \(M\) be a complete manifold without boundary, Ric\((M) \geq - k , k \geq 0\text{.}\) Let \(H (x, y, t)\) be the fundamental solution of the heat equation

\begin{equation*} \left( \Delta - \frac{\partial }{\partial t} \right) u (x, t) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then for any \(\delta \in (0, 1)\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} H(x, y, t) \leq C(\delta, n) V^{-\frac{1}{2}} _x ( \sqrt{t}) V_y ^{-\frac{1}{2}} ( \sqrt{t}) \exp \left( - \frac{r^2 (x, y)}{(4 + \delta)t} + C_1 \delta k t\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

For the proof, see the book of Yau and Schoen. We give some applications of the above theorem.

Theorem 4.1.5. Gromov.

Let \(M\) be a compact manifold without boundary, let Ric\((M) \geq 0\) and \(d\) be the diameter of \(M\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \lambda _k \geq \frac{C(n)}{d^2} ( k + 1)^{\frac{2}{n}} \end{equation*}

where \(\lambda _k\) is the \(kth\) eigenvalue of the manifold.

Proof.

We set \(x = y\) and \(k = 0\) in the above theorem. Then we have

\begin{equation*} H(x, x, t) \leq C(n) V^{-1} _x (\sqrt{t})\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since

\begin{equation*} H(x, y, t)= \sum^\infty_{k=1} e ^{-\lambda k t} f_k (x) f _k (y) \end{equation*}

we have

\begin{equation*} H(x, x, t)= \sum^\infty_{k=1} e ^{-\lambda _{k} t}\text{.} \end{equation*}

As a result we have

\begin{equation*} \sum^\infty_{k=1} e ^{-\lambda k t} \leq C(n) \int_M V^{-1} _x (\sqrt{t}) \, dx\text{.} \end{equation*}

If \(\sqrt{t} \geq d\text{,}\) then \(V^{-1} _x (\sqrt{x}) = { vol} (M)\text{,}\) if \(\sqrt{t} \leq d\text{,}\) then by the Bishop volume comparison theorem

\begin{equation*} \frac{V_x (\sqrt{t}}{ V _x (d)} \geq \left( \frac{\sqrt{t}}{d}\right) ^n\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus

\begin{equation} \sum^\infty_{k=1} e ^{-\lambda _{k} t} \leq C(n)\left\{ \begin{array}{ll} \left(\frac{d}{\sqrt{t}}\right) ^n \amp t \leq d^2\\ 1 \amp t > d^2 \end{array} \right. .\tag{$\star$}\tag{4.1.1} \end{equation}

For fixed \(k\text{,}\) by the monotonicity, we have

\begin{equation*} ke ^{-\lambda _{k} t} \leq C(n)\left\{ \begin{array}{ll} \left(\frac{d}{\sqrt{t}}\right) ^n \amp t \leq d^2\\ 1 \amp t > d^2 \end{array} \right.\text{.} \end{equation*}

We let

\begin{equation*} \sqrt{t} = k ^{-\frac{1}{n}} d \end{equation*}

and the result follows.

We can compare the above eigenvalue estimate with the result of Cheng-Li.

From (\(\star\)), we can get

\begin{equation*} \sum^\infty_{k=1} e ^{- \lambda _{k} t} \leq C t ^{-\frac{n}{2}}\;\; t \leq d^2 \end{equation*}

where \(C\) is the absolute \(d\) constant. On the other hand, if we consider

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial }{\partial t} \int_M H (x, y, t) ^2 d y \amp = \amp 2 \int_M H (x, y, t) \Delta H (x , y, t) \, dy\\ \amp =\amp - 2 \int | \nabla y H (x, y, t) | ^2 \, dy\\ \amp \leq \amp - 2 C \left( \int_M |H (x, y, t) | ^{\frac{2n}{n-2}} \, dy \right) ^{\frac{n-2}{n}} \end{align*}

where \(C\) is the Sobolev constant. Since for any \(t > 0\)

\begin{equation*} \int H (x, z, t) \, d z \leq 1\text{.} \end{equation*}

So we have

\begin{equation*} \left|\int _M |H | (x, y, t) | ^{\frac{2n}{n-2}} \, dy \right| ^{\frac{n-2}{n}}\geq \left( \int_M |H (x, y, t) | ^{2} \, dy \right) ^{\frac{2+n}{n}} \end{equation*}

Proof.

Let \(f = H (x, y, t)\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \int f ^2 = \int f ^{\frac{2n}{n+2}+ \frac{4}{n+2}}\leq \left( \int f^{\frac{2n}{n-2}}\right) ^{\frac{n+2}{2n}}\left. \left( \int f \right) ^{\frac{4}{n+2}}\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial }{\partial t}\int _M H(x, y, t)^2 \, dy \leq - C \left( \int_M |H (x, y, t) ^2 | dy \right) ^{\frac{2+n}{n}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since

\begin{equation*} \lim_{t \rightarrow 0} H (x, x, t) = \infty\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus

\begin{equation*} H (x, x, 2t) \leq \left( \frac{4}{n} Ct \right) ^{-\frac{n}{2}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Using the same method as before, we have

\begin{equation*} \lambda _k \geq C \left( \frac{k}{{ vol} (M)}\right) ^{\frac{2}{n}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The second application of the heat kernel estimate is the following resolvent estimate.

Theorem 4.1.6.

For any \(\beta > 0\) there is \(n \in N, \alpha \lt 0 , C \subset \infty\) such that the integral kernel \(g _{\alpha} ^{(\frac{n}{2})}(x, y)\) of \((\Delta - \alpha) ^{-\frac{n}{2}}\) satisfies.

\begin{equation*} g _{\alpha} ^{(\frac{n}{2})}(x, y) \leq C \phi (x) ^2 e ^{-\beta d(x, y)} \end{equation*}

where \(\phi (x) = (v (B_1 (x)) ^{-\frac{1}{2}}\text{.}\)

Proof.

First, using the heat kernel \(H (x, t)\) we can write

\begin{equation*} g _{\alpha} ^{(\frac{n}{2})}(x, y) = C _2 \int ^\infty_0 H (x, y, t) t ^{ \frac{n}{2}-1 } e ^{\alpha t} \, dt\text{.} \end{equation*}

By the volume comparison theorem, we have

\begin{align*} v ^{ \frac{n}{2}} (B_{\sqrt{t}}(x)) v ^{ \frac{-1}{2} }(B_{\sqrt{t}}(y))\amp \leq \amp C_2 \phi (x)^2 \sup (1, t ^{-\frac{n}{2}})\\ \amp \times \amp e ^{\beta _{2} d (x, y)}\text{.} \end{align*}

By an element calculation, for any \(\beta _3 > 0\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \exp \left( - \frac{d^2 (x, y)}{C_1 4t} \right) \leq \exp ( \beta_3 d (x, y) ) \exp (C_1 \beta ^2_3 t )\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus for any \(\beta > 0\text{,}\) there exists \(\alpha \lt 0 , C_3 \lt \infty\) such that

\begin{equation*} H (x, y, t) \leq C_3 \phi ^2 (x) e ^{-\beta _{2} d (x, y)} \sup \{ t ^{ -\frac{n}{2} } , 1 \} t ^{ - (\alpha + 1 ) t} \end{equation*}

which implies

\begin{equation*} g _{\alpha} ^{(\frac{n}{2})} (x, y) \leq C_t \phi ^2 (x) e ^{-\beta d (x, y)}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Cheeger-Yau's heat kernel comparison theorem.

Theorem 4.1.7.

Let \(M\) be a complete Riemannian manifold such that Ric\((M) \geq 0\text{.}\) Fixing \(x \in M , r_0 > 0\text{.}\) The heat kernel \(H (x, y, t)\) in \(B(x, r_0)\) and the heat kernel \(\varepsilon (r (x, y) , t)\) in \(V (k, r_0)\) satisfies the following

\begin{equation*} \varepsilon (r (x, y) , t) \leq H (x, y, t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

(For both Dirichlet and Neumann conditions).

Proof.

Using the property of the heat kernel, we have

\begin{align*} \lefteqn{H(x, y, t) - \varepsilon (x, y, t)}\\ \amp \amp = \int ^t _0 \int _{B (x_1 r_0)} \frac{d}{ds} ( \varepsilon (x, z, t - s) H (z, y, s) ) \, d z ds\\ \amp \amp = - \int ^t _0 \int _{B (x_1 r_0) }\left( \frac{d}{ds}\varepsilon (r (x, z), t-s)\right) H(z, y, s) \, d z ds\\ \amp \amp + \int^t_0 \int _{B (x_1 r_0) }\varepsilon (r (x, z), t-s)\frac{d}{ds} H(z, y, s) \, d z ds\\ \amp \amp = - \int^t_0 \int _{B (x_1 r_0)} \tilde{\Delta} \varepsilon (r (x, z), t - s) H(z, y, s) \, d z ds\\ \amp \amp + \int^t_0 \int _{B (x_1 r_0)}\varepsilon (r (x, z), t - s) \Delta H(z, y, s) \, d z ds\text{.} \end{align*}

Using the Green's formula, under either the Dirichlet or Neumann boundary condition, we have

\begin{align*} \lefteqn{\int _{B (x_1 r_0)}\varepsilon (r (x, z), t - s) \Delta H(z, y, s) \, d z}\\ \amp \amp = \int _{B (x_1 r_0)}\Delta \varepsilon (r (x, z), t - s) H(z, y, s) \, d z\text{.} \end{align*}

Since \(H(\xi, y, s) > 0\text{,}\) we just need to prove that

\begin{equation*} \tilde{\Delta} \varepsilon (r (x, z), t - s)\leq \Delta \varepsilon (r (x, z), t - s)\text{.} \end{equation*}

This essentially follows from the Laplacian comparison theorem: Let \(x = (r, \zeta ) , \zeta \in S^{n-1}\text{.}\) Then

\begin{align*} \tilde{\Delta } \amp = \amp \frac{\partial ^2}{\partial r^2} + m (r) \frac{\partial }{\partial r} , m (r) \frac{\partial }{\partial r} \log { det} \sqrt{\tilde{g}}\\ \Delta \amp = \amp \frac{\partial ^2}{\partial r^2} + m (r,\zeta) \frac{\partial }{\partial r} , m (r,\zeta) \frac{\partial }{\partial r} \log { det} \sqrt{{g}}\text{.} \end{align*}

Since Ric\((M) \geq (n-1)_k\text{,}\) using the volume comparison theorem, we have

\begin{equation*} m (r, \zeta ) \leq m (r)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial \varepsilon}{\partial r} \lt 0 \end{equation*}

we have

\begin{equation*} \tilde{\Delta} \varepsilon (r, t-s) \leq \Delta \varepsilon (r, t - s)\text{.} \end{equation*}

In the above proof, we didn't take the cut-locus into account. Using some kind of limiting process, we can overcome the difficulty.

Theorem 4.1.8.

Let \(M\) be a complete Riemannian manifold such that \(Ricci (M) \geq (n-1) k , n = \dim M\text{.}\) We use \(B (x_0, r)\) to denote the ball centered at \(x _0\) with radius \(r\text{.}\) Let \(V (k, r)\) be the ball of radius \(r\) in a simply connected space form. Then with the Dirichlet boundary condition, we have

\begin{equation*} \lambda _1 (B (x_0, r)) \leq \lambda _1 (V (k, r))\text{.} \end{equation*}

\begin{proof} Let \(H(x, y, t)\) and \(\varepsilon (x, y, t)\) be the corresponding heat kernel. Then we have

\begin{align*} H (x, y, t) \amp =\amp \sum e^{-\lambda _1t} \phi ^2 _1 (x)\\ \varepsilon (x, y, t) \amp = \amp \sum e^{-\tilde{\lambda} _1t} \tilde{\phi} ^2 _1 (x)\text{.} \end{align*}

If we let \(t \rightarrow \infty\text{,}\) we get \(\lambda _1 \leq \tilde{\lambda}_1\text{.}\) S.Y. Cheng concretely computed the upper bounds of the eigenvalue

  1. if Ric\((M) \geq 0\text{,}\) then \(\lambda _1 \leq \frac{2n(n+4)}{d^2}\)

  2. if Ric\((M) \geq n-1\text{,}\) then \(\lambda _1 \leq n\frac{\pi^2}{d^2}\)

  3. if Ric\((M) \geq (n-1) (-k)\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \leq \frac{1}{4} k + \frac{C_n}{d^2} \;\;\; C_k = 2n (n+4)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Unfortunately, for Neumann boundary condition, we don't have the comparison theorem for the 1st eigenvalues directly. Under, the Neumann boundary condition, the first eigenvalues are always 0. The comparison theorem for that is trivial.

It is thus interesting to have the following result.

Theorem 4.1.9.

If \(M\) is a compact manifold without boundary, then

\begin{equation*} \lambda _1 (M) \leq \lambda _1 \left(V \left(k, \frac{d}{2}\right)\right) \end{equation*}

where \(d\) is the diameter of \(M\text{.}\)

Proof of a theorem of Brascamp and Lieb.

Theorem 4.1.10.

Let \(\Omega\) be a bounded convex domain of \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\) Let \(u\) be the first Dirichlet eigenfunction. Let

\begin{equation*} \Delta u = - \lambda , u \;\; u > 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then \(\log u\) must be concave.

Proof.

We choose any function \(u_0 > 0 , u _0 |_{\partial \Omega} = 0\) such that \(- \log u_0\) is concave. Such a function always exists. For example, we can take the convex hull of the graph of \(- \log u\text{.}\)

Consider the flow

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \Delta u + \lambda _i u \;\; \frac{u}{\partial \Omega} = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

We assume that \(u_t \rightarrow u\text{,}\) the first eigenfunction. We are going to use the maximum principle. Let \(T\) be the biggest number such that det\((- \Delta ^2 \log u)\) is degenerated. Thus there is an \(x_0 \in M\) and a direction \(i\) such that

\begin{equation*} - ( \log u) _{ii} (x_0) = 0 \end{equation*}

and for other \(j, (- \log u) _{jj} (x_0) \geq 0\text{.}\) Let \(\phi = - \log u\text{.}\) Then the envolution of \(\phi\) is

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} = \Delta \phi - |\nabla \phi |^2 - \lambda _1\text{.} \end{equation*}

By the maximum principle, \(\phi_{iik} = 0 , \frac{\partial \phi_{ii}}{\partial t} \leq 0 \Delta \phi _{ii} \geq 0\text{.}\) Thus

\begin{align*} 0 \geq \frac{\partial \phi_{ii}}{\partial t} \amp = \amp \Delta \phi_{ii} -2 \phi _k \phi_{kii} - 2 \phi ^2_{ki}\\ \amp \geq \amp - 2 \phi_{ki}^2\text{.} \end{align*}

However, by convexity, \(\phi_{ki}^2 \leq \phi_{ii} \cdot \phi_{kk}=0, \phi_{ki} \equiv 0\text{.}\) The theorem follows from strong maximum principle.

Let \(\Omega\) be a bounded domain with smooth boundary. Let \(u\) be the first Dirichlet eigenfunction with eigenvalue \(\lambda _1\text{.}\) Then

Lemma 4.1.11.

\(u \geq 0\text{.}\)

Proof.

Otherwise, we may use \(|u|\) in place of \(u\text{.}\) From the Kato's inequality

\begin{equation*} |\nabla |u|| \leq |\nabla u| \end{equation*}

we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{\int |\nabla |u| |^2}{\int u^2} \leq \frac{\int |\nabla u|^2}{\int u^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(\frac{\int |\nabla u|^2}{\int u^2}\) is minimal, we must have

\begin{equation*} \frac{\int |\nabla |u| |^2}{\int u^2} = \frac{\int |\nabla u|^2}{\int u^2} \end{equation*}

and thus \(u\) is an eigenfunction.

Lemma 4.1.12.

\(u > 0\text{.}\)

If not, assume that \(u (x_0) = 0\text{.}\) Let

\begin{equation*} u(x) = p^N (x) + 0 ( x ^{N+\varepsilon} )\text{.} \end{equation*}

By the equation \(\Delta u = - \lambda_1 u\text{,}\) we must have

\begin{equation*} \Delta p^N (x) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since \(u (x) \geq 0\text{,}\) we have \(p^N (x) \geq 0\text{.}\) This contradicts to the maximum principle.

Lemma 4.1.13.

\(\frac{\partial u}{\partial n} \neq 0\text{.}\)

Proof.

Using Taylor's expansion

\begin{equation*} u(x) = p ^N (x) + 0 ( x ^{N+ \epsilon)} )\text{.} \end{equation*}

Again, \(\Delta p^N (x) = 0 , p^N (x) > 0\) in \(\Omega\text{.}\) By the Hopf lemma

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial u}{\partial n} = \frac{\partial p^N}{\partial n} \neq 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Consider the flow

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \Delta u = \lambda _1 u, \frac{u}{\partial \Omega} = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

We have

Lemma 4.1.14.

For any smooth initial \(u _0 > 0\text{,}\) the flow exists and converges to the first eigenfunction.

Proof.

Let

\begin{equation*} u_0 = \sum a _i f _i (x)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then

\begin{equation*} u ( t, x) = \sum a_i e ^{(-a_{i} - \lambda _{1} )t} f_i (x) \end{equation*}

solves the flow equation. Obviously,

\begin{equation*} \lim_{t \rightarrow \infty} u(t, x) = a _i f_1 (x)\text{.} \end{equation*}

If we choose the \(u_0\) such that \(a _i \neq 0\text{.}\) Then the flow converges to the first eigenfunction.

Lemma 4.1.15.

If \(u_0 > 0\text{,}\) then \(u (t, x) >0\text{.}\)

Proof.

Maximum principle.

Lemma 4.1.16.

If \(\Omega\) is convex, then near \(\partial \Omega (-\log u)\) is convex.

Proof.

We solve \(u ( x_1 \ldots, x_n) = 0\) to get

\begin{equation*} x _n = x_n (x _1 , \ldots , x _{n-1} ) \end{equation*}

\(\Omega\) is convex

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial ^ 2 x _n }{\partial x_i \partial x_j} > 0 \;\;\;\mbox{(positive definite matrix)}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Using implicitly function theorem, we have

\begin{equation*} -\frac{u_{ij}}{u_n} + \frac{u_{in} u_j}{u^2_n} + \frac{u_{jn} u_i}{u^2_n} - \frac{u_i u_j u_{nn}}{u_n^3} > 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus for any \((a _1, \cdots , a_n)\) if \(\sum a _i u_i = 0\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} - u _{ij} a _i a_j \geq \varepsilon |a|^2\text{.} \end{equation*}

For any \(a + \mu b , b = (u _1 \ldots, u_n )\text{,}\) we have

\begin{align*} \amp - \amp u_{ij} (a_i + \mu b_i )( a_j + \mu b_j )\\ \amp \amp + \frac{1}{u} |\mu | ^2 |b| ^4 > 0 \text{.} \end{align*}
