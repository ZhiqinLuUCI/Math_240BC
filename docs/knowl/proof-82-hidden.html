Define

\begin{equation*} s=\alpha t\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\alpha>1\) is a constant to be determined. By assumption

\begin{equation*} \fint_Q f^q(x) dx=1\leq s^q\text{.} \end{equation*}

We use the function \(f(x)\) and the number \(s^q\) to perform the Calderón-Zygmund decomposition. We then define a sequence of cubes \(Q_j\subset Q\) such that

  1. each side of \(Q_j\) is parallel to a side of \(Q\text{;}\) the interior of \(Q_j\) are disjoint;

  2. \begin{equation} s^q\lt \fint_{Q_j} f^q(x) dx\leq 2^n s^q\text{.}\tag{10.1.2} \end{equation}

  3. for a.e. \(x\not\in \bigcup_j Q_j\text{,}\) we have \(f(x)\leq s\text{.}\)

Briefly speaking, the sequences can be defined inductively in the following way: we first divide \(Q\) into \(2^n\) sub-cubes like in Figure (ref fig2)

Let \(Q'\) be one of the sub-cubes. Then

\begin{equation*} \fint_{Q'} f^q(x) dx\leq \frac{1}{m(Q')}\int_Q f^q(x)dx\leq 2^n s^q\text{.} \end{equation*}

If

\begin{equation*} \fint_{Q'} f^q(x) dx>s^q\text{,} \end{equation*}

we say \(Q'\) is “good” and we assign \(Q'\) as one of the \(Q_j\)'s. If \(Q'\) is not “good”, then since

\begin{equation*} \fint_{Q'} f^q(x) dx\leq s^q\text{,} \end{equation*}

we can repeat the division as above. In this way, we get a sequence \(\{Q_j\}\) such that they satisfy  (10.1.2). Now for all \(x\not\in \bigcup_j Q_j\text{,}\) there is a sequence \(Q_j''\) shrinking to \(x\) such that

\begin{equation*} \fint_{Q_j''} f^q(x)dx\leq s^q\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus by the Lebesgue Theorem, for a.e. \(x\text{,}\) \(f(x)\leq s\text{.}\)

We define

\begin{equation*} G=\bigcup_jQ_j\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then we have

\begin{equation*} \int_{E(s)} f^q(x) dx=\int_G f^q(x)dx=\sum_j\int_{Q_j} f^q(x) dx\leq 2^n s^q m(G)\text{.} \end{equation*}

On the other hand, let \(x\in Q_j\) and let \(r={ diam}(Q_j)\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{m(B(x,r))}\int_{B(x,r)} f^q(x) dx\geq \frac{1}{m(B(x,r))}\, m(Q_j) s^q\geq c_1 s^q\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(c_1=c_1(n)>0\) is a constant. By the definition of the maximum function, we have

\begin{equation*} M(f^q,x)\geq\fint_{B(x,r)} f^q(x) dx\geq c_1 s^q\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus by the assumption of the theorem, we have

\begin{equation*} M(f,x)\geq (c_1/b)^{1/q} s=(c_1/b)^{1/q} \alpha t>\beta t\text{.} \end{equation*}

If we choose \(\alpha\) large enough, we can assume that \(\beta>1\text{.}\) By definition of the maximum function, for any \(x\text{,}\) there is an \(r_x>0\) such that

\begin{equation*} \fint_{B(x,r_x)} f(x)dx\geq \beta t\text{.} \end{equation*}

Such a collection of balls \(B(x,r_x)\) covers \(Q_j\text{.}\) Thus by the Vitali Covering Theorem, there is a countable subset of balls \(B_j\) which are disjoint and the union of \(5B_j\) covers \(Q\text{.}\) We therefore have the inequality

\begin{equation*} m(G)\leq 5^n\sum_j m(B_j)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus we have

\begin{equation*} \beta tm(B_j)\leq\int_{B_j} f(x) dx\leq\int_{B_j\cap E(t)} f(x) dx+tm(B_j)\text{.} \end{equation*}

We therefore have

\begin{equation*} m(B_j)\leq\frac{1}{(\beta-1) t}\int_{B_j\cap E(t)} f(x) dx\text{.} \end{equation*}
