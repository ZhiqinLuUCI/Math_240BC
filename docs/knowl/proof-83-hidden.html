Define

\begin{equation*} h(t)=\int_{E(t)} f(x) dx\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then it is easy to verify that

\begin{equation*} \int_{E(t)} f^r(x) dx=-\int_t^\infty s^{r-1} dh(s)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus the lemma can be written as

\begin{equation} -\int_t^\infty s^{q-1} dh(s)\leq at^{q-1} h(t)\text{.}\tag{10.1.3} \end{equation}

Define

\begin{equation*} I(r)=-\int_1^\infty t^{r-1}dh(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

and for \(p>q>0\text{,}\) define

\begin{equation*} J=(p-q)\int_1^\infty t^{p-q-1}\left(-\int_t^\infty s^{q-1}dh(s)\right) dt\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then byÂ (10.1.3), we have

\begin{equation*} J\leq (p-q)\int_1^\infty at^{p-2} h(t) dt=\frac{a(p-q)}{p-1}\int_1^\infty h(t) dt^{p-1}= \frac{a(p-q)}{p-1}(-I(1)+I(p))\text{.} \end{equation*}

On the other hand, we use integration by parts to get

\begin{equation*} J=\int_1^\infty\left(-\int_t^\infty s^{q-1}dh(s)\right)dt^{p-q}=I(p)-I(q)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus we get

\begin{equation*} I(p)-I(q)\leq \frac{a(p-q)}{p-1}(-I(1)+I(p))\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus for \(p\) sufficiently close to \(q\text{,}\) we get

\begin{equation*} I(p)\leq C(I(q)+I(1))\text{.} \end{equation*}
