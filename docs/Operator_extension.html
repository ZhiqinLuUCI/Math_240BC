Section 2.2 Self-adjoint extension of the Laplace operator

In this section, we assume that \(M\) is Riemannian manifold, not necessarily compact nor complete.

Let \(\Lambda^p(M)\) be the space of smooth \(p\)-forms and let \(L^2(\Lambda^p(M))\) be the metric space completion of \(\Lambda^p(M)\) under the inner product product defined in ¬†(2.1.1).

Checkpoint 2.2.1.

Prove that we can identify \(L^2(\Lambda^p(M))\) to be the space of \(p\)-forms \(\omega\text{,}\) where

\begin{equation*} \omega=a_{i_1\cdots i_p}\omega_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge\omega_{i_p}\text{,} \end{equation*}

such that \(a_{i_1\cdots i_p}\) are locally \(L^2\) integrable functions.

The reason we would like to use \(L^2(\Lambda^p(M))\) in stead of \(\Lambda^p(M)\) is, of course, that \(L^2(\Lambda^p(M))\) is complete, allowing many applications in analysis.

Unfortunately, it is not possible to extend \(\Delta\) to \(L^2(\Lambda^p(M))\) as a symmetric linear operator. In what follows, we briefly explain the reason.

First, the Laplace operator is not a bounded operator on \(\Lambda^p(M)\text{.}\) We can give counterexamples even in one-dimensional case: let \(f\) be a smooth function on \([0,1]\text{.}\) Then there doesn't exist a constant \(C\) such that

\begin{equation*} \int_0^1(f''(t))^2 dt\leq C\int_0^1(f(t))^2 dt\text{.} \end{equation*}

Second, like most differential operators, the Laplace operator is a closed-graph operator. That is, if \(\eta_j\to \eta\) and \(\Delta\eta_j\to\eta'\) in \(L^2\) and \(\eta\in\Lambda^p(M)\text{,}\) then we must have \(\eta'=\Delta\eta\text{.}\) To see this, we consider any smooth \(p\) form \(\omega\text{:}\) We have

\begin{equation*} \int\langle\eta'-\Delta\eta,\omega\rangle=\lim_{j\to\infty}\int \langle\Delta\eta_j,\omega\rangle-\lim_{j\to\infty}\int\langle\eta_j,\Delta\omega\rangle=0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus we have \(\eta'=\Delta\eta\text{.}\)

If \(\Delta\) could be extended to a linear operator of \(L^2(\Lambda^p(M))\text{,}\) then by the Closed Graph Theorem, \(\Delta\) could have been bounded, which is a contradiction.

Because of the above result, the best we can do is to extend the Laplacian operator into a densely defined self-adjoint operator. Of course, \(\Lambda^p(M)\) is dense in \(L^2(\Lambda^p(M))\text{.}\) But for such an operator (i.e, \(\Delta\) and its domain \({\mathcal Dom}(\Delta))\text{,}\) we don't have the so-called spectral theorem. So in order to get meaningful results, we have to extend the Laplace operator first.

Definition 2.2.2.

Let \(H\) be a Hilbert space, Given a densely defined linear operator \(A\) on \(H\text{,}\) its adjoint \(A^*\) is defined as follows:

  1. The domain \({\mathcal Dom}(A^*)\) of \(A^*\) consists of vectors \(x\) in \(H\) such that

    \begin{equation*} y\mapsto \langle x, Ay\rangle \end{equation*}

    is a bounded linear functional, where \(y\in{\mathcal Dom}(A)\text{;}\)

  2. By the Riesz Representation Theorem for linear functionals, if \(x\) is in the domain of \(A^*\text{,}\) there is a unique vector \(z\) in \(H\) such that

    \begin{equation*} \langle x,Ay\rangle=\langle z,y\rangle \end{equation*}

    for any \(y\in {\mathcal Dom}(A)\text{.}\) This vector \(z\) is defined to be \(A^*x\text{.}\) It can be shown that the dependence of \(z\) on \(x\) is linear.

If \(A^*=A\) (which implies that \({\mathcal Dom}\, (A^*)={\mathcal Dom}\,(A)\)), then \(A\) is called self-adjoint.

For a (densely-defined) self-adjoint operator \(A\text{,}\) we have the Spectral Theorem. That is, there is a spectral measure such that

\begin{equation*} A=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\lambda dE\text{.} \end{equation*}

A densely defined self-adjoint operator \(\bar\Delta\) is called an extension of \(\Delta\text{,}\) if \({\mathcal Dom}\, (\Delta)\subset {\mathcal Dom}\, (\bar\Delta)\text{,}\) and

\begin{equation*} \Delta=\bar\Delta\,|_{{\mathcal Dom}\, (\Delta)}\text{.} \end{equation*}

For the rest of the section, we shall study the extensions of \(\Delta\text{.}\) We shall prove that the extension of \(\Delta\) always exists, but in general, they are not unique in general.

Define \(H^1(M)\) to be the Sobolev space of the completion of \(\Lambda^p(M)\) under the norm

\begin{equation*} ||\eta||_1=\sqrt{\int_M|\eta|^2 dV_M}+\sqrt{\int_M|\nabla\eta|^2\, dV_M}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Similarly, let \(\Lambda_0^p(M)\) be the space of smooth \(p\)-forms with compact support and define \(H_0^1(M)\) to be the Sobolev space of the completion of \(\Lambda_0^p(M)\) under the above norm. Then we have

\begin{equation*} H_0^1(M)\subset H^1(M)\text{.} \end{equation*}

In general, the above two spaces are not equal. However, we have

Theorem 2.2.3.

If \(M\) is a complete Riemannian manifold, then

\begin{equation*} H_0^1(M)= H^1(M)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Proof.

Let \(\phi\in H^1(M)\text{.}\) Then there exists a sequence \(\{\phi_j\}\in \Lambda^p(M)\) such that

\begin{equation*} \phi_j\to \phi, j\to\infty \end{equation*}

in the \(\|\|_1\) norm.

Let \(p\in M\) be a fixed point and let \(\rho\) be a smooth function on \(\mathbb R\) with compact support such that in a neighborhood of \(0\text{,}\) \(\rho\) is the constant \(1\text{.}\) Let \(d(x,y)\) be the distance function. Define

\begin{equation*} \rho_j(x)=\rho(j^{-1}\,d(p,x))\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then we can prove that

\begin{equation*} \rho_j\,\phi_j\to\phi \end{equation*}

in the \(\|\|_1\) norm.

Checkpoint 2.2.4.

Provide the detailed proof of the above theorem.

We define the quadratic form \(Q\) by

\begin{equation*} Q(\omega,\eta)=\int_M\left(\langle d\omega,d\eta \rangle +\langle\delta\omega,\delta\eta\rangle\right) dV_M \end{equation*}

for any \(\omega,\eta\in H_0^1(M)\text{.}\) By the Weitzenb√∂ck formula, we have

Proposition 2.2.5.

If the curvature is bounded, then \(\sqrt{Q(\phi,\phi)+\|\phi\|_{L^2}^2}\) is equivalent to the \(\|\phi\|_1\text{.}\)

The Friedrichs extension \(\bar\Delta\) of \(\Delta\) is defined by the following. Let

\begin{align*} \amp {\mathcal Dom}(\bar \Delta)=\left\{\phi\in H_0^1(M)\mid\right.\\ \amp \left.\forall\psi\in \Lambda^p(M), \exists f\in L^2(\Lambda^p(M)), s.t.\, Q(\phi,\psi)=( f,\psi)\right\}\text{.} \end{align*}

Lemma 2.2.6.

Using the above notations, we have

\begin{equation*} {\mathcal Dom}(\bar\Delta)={\mathcal Dom}(\bar\Delta^*)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Proof.

For any \(\phi,\psi\in{\mathcal Dom}(\bar\Delta)\text{,}\) \((\bar\Delta\phi,\psi)=Q(\psi,\phi)=(\phi,\bar\Delta\psi)\) is a bounded functional. Thus \(\phi\in {\mathcal Dom}(\bar\Delta^*)\text{.}\) On the other hand, if \(\phi\in {\mathcal Dom}(\Delta^*)\text{,}\) then the functional \(\psi\mapsto (\Delta\psi,\phi)\) is bounded. By the Riesz representation theorem, there is a unique \(f\in L^2(\Lambda^p(M))\) such that \((\bar\Delta\psi,\phi)=(f,\psi)\text{.}\) Thus we must have \(Q(\phi,\psi)=(\bar\Delta\psi,\phi)=(f,\psi)\text{,}\) and \(\phi\in {\mathcal Dom}(\bar\Delta)\text{.}\)

Theorem 2.2.7.

The Laplace operator has a self-adjoint extension.

Theorem 2.2.8.

Let \(M\) be a complete Riemannian manifold. Then the extension of the Laplace operator is unique.

Proof.

Let \(\Delta_2\) be another self-adjoint extension of \(\Delta\text{.}\) Then for \(\phi\in {\mathcal Dom}(\bar\Delta)\) the functional

\begin{equation*} \psi\mapsto(\phi,\Delta\psi)+(\phi,\psi)=(\Delta_2\phi,\psi)+(\phi,\psi) \end{equation*}

for \(\psi\in\Lambda_0^p(M)\) is bounded under the norm \(\|\|_1\text{.}\) By Proposition¬†2.2.5, it is also bounded under the norm

\begin{equation*} \sqrt{Q(\cdot,\cdot)+(\cdot,\cdot)}\text{.} \end{equation*}

By the Riesz representation theorem, there exists an element \(\mu\in H^1(M)\) such that

\begin{equation*} (\phi,\Delta\psi)+(\phi,\psi)=Q(\mu,\psi)+(\mu,\psi)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Therefore \(\phi=\psi\in H^1(M)\text{.}\) Since \(M\) is complete, By Theorem¬†2.2.3, \(\phi\in H_0^1(M)\text{.}\) For any \(\psi\in\Lambda_0^p(M)\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} (\Delta_2\phi,\psi)=(\phi,\Delta\psi)=Q(\phi,\psi) \end{equation*}

and by taking limit, the above equality is also true for any \(\psi\in H_0^1(M)\text{.}\) Thus we prove

\begin{equation*} {\mathcal Dom}\,(\Delta_2)\subset {\mathcal Dom}\,(\bar\Delta)\text{.} \end{equation*}

On the other hands, since both \(\bar\Delta\) and \(\Delta_2\) are self-adjoint, we have

\begin{equation*} {\mathcal Dom}\,(\bar\Delta)={\mathcal Dom}\,(\bar\Delta^*)\subset{\mathcal Dom}\,(\Delta_2^*)={\mathcal Dom}\,(\Delta_2)\text{,} \end{equation*}

and thus we prove

\begin{equation*} {\mathcal Dom}\,(\bar\Delta)={\mathcal Dom}\,(\Delta_2)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus \(\Delta_2=\bar\Delta\text{,}\) and the extension is unique.

In general, the self-adjoint extension is not unique.

Example 2.2.9.

Let \(M\) be a compact orientable manifold with smooth boundary. Let \(\mathcal C_0^\infty(M)\) be the space of smooth functions whose supports are within the interior of \(M\text{,}\) and let \(\mathcal C^\infty(M)\) be the space of smooth functions (smooth up to the boundary). We can define two kinds of Laplacians: the first one \(\Delta_D\) is called the Dirichlet Laplacian, which is the same as the Friedrichs extension \(\bar\Delta\text{.}\) The second one \(\Delta_N\) is called the Neumann Laplacian, defined by

\begin{align*} \amp {\mathcal Dom}(\Delta_N)=\left\{\phi\in H^1(M)\mid\right.\\ \amp \left.\forall\psi\in \mathcal C^\infty(M) \,\,s.t. \frac{\pa\psi}{\pa n}=0, \exists f\in L^2(M), s.t.\, Q(\phi,\psi)=( f,\psi)\right\}\text{.} \end{align*}

For \(\phi,\psi\in \mathcal C^\infty(M)\text{,}\) we have the Green's formula

\begin{equation*} (\Delta\phi,\psi)=\int_{\pa M}\frac{\pa\phi}{\pa n}\,\psi-\int_M\phi\,\frac{\pa\psi}{\pa n}+(\phi,\Delta\psi)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Therefore, if \(\phi\in {\mathcal Dom}(\Delta_D)\cap\mathcal C^\infty(M)\text{,}\) we have \(\phi|_{\pa M}=0\text{,}\) and if \(\phi\in{\mathcal Dom}(\Delta_N)\cap \mathcal C^\infty(M)\text{,}\) we have \(\frac{\pa\phi}{\pa n}=0\text{.}\) These justify the names.

Many results related to the Laplace operator are true even on noncomplete manifolds.

Theorem 2.2.10.

Let \(f\in { ker}\,(\bar\Delta)\text{.}\) Assume that \(f\in L^2(M)\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is a constant.

This is a theorem of Yau in the case of complete manifold. But we shall prove that it is also true for any noncompete manifold.

Proof.

Let \(\rho_j\) be the smooth function defined in Theorem¬†2.2.3. Then we have

\begin{equation*} 0=(\bar\Delta f, \rho_j^2 f)=Q(f,\rho_j^2 f)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus we have

\begin{equation*} \int_M\rho_j^2|\nabla f|^2=-2\int_M \rho_j\, f\nabla\rho_j\nabla f\leq 2\sqrt{\int_M|\nabla \rho_j|^2 f^2\, dV_M} \cdot\sqrt{\int_M\rho_j^2|\nabla f|^2\, dV_M}\text{.} \end{equation*}

It follows that

\begin{equation*} \int_M\rho_j^2|\nabla f|^2\,dV_M\leq \frac{C}{j^2}\int_Mf^2\, dV_M\to 0 \end{equation*}

and the theorem is proved.

Example 2.2.11.

Let \(M=\mathbb R^n-\{0\}\text{.}\) Then any \(L^2\) harmonic function extends to the origin. Thus by the theorem of Yau, we know that it has to be a constant. Let \(M\) be a bounded domain. Then the fact that \(f\) is in the domain of the Laplace operator implies that \(f\) vanishes on the boundary. Hence \(f\) is zero.
